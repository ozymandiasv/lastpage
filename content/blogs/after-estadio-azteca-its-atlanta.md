Perhaps the greatest intercontinental rivalry in international football returns tonight. Argentina and England meet once again, this time in Atlanta, with a place in the 2026 FIFA World Cup final at stake. It has been twenty-four years since they last met at a World Cup, and every time these two nations share a pitch, history somehow finds its way back into the conversation. This rivalry is much older than most people realise. Football itself arrived in Argentina during the late nineteenth century through Alexander Watson Hutton, the Scottish schoolteacher widely regarded as the father of Argentine football. What began as England introducing the sport to Argentina slowly evolved into one of the fiercest rivalries the game has ever witnessed.
## Before Maradona, There Was Rattín
For many people, the rivalry begins with Diego Maradona, but I think it truly began in 1966. During the World Cup quarter-final, Argentina captain Antonio Rattín was controversially sent off for what the referee interpreted as dissent, years before football introduced yellow and red cards. Rattín refused to leave the pitch, arguing that he had done nothing wrong. As he walked off, he famously picked up one of the chocolates thrown from the crowd, ate it, wiped his hands on an English flag, and even sat briefly on the red carpet reserved for the British royal family. England eventually won the match against a ten-man Argentina, but the controversy refused to disappear. In many ways, that afternoon changed football forever. It is widely believed that incidents like the Rattín controversy inspired FIFA referee Ken Aston to introduce the yellow and red card system that every football fan now takes for granted.
## Maradona Changed Everything
Then came the Falklands War, a conflict that transformed a football rivalry into something much more emotional for both nations. Four years later, football produced one of its most unforgettable afternoons. The 1986 World Cup quarter-final gave us Diego Maradona at his absolute peak. In the space of a few minutes, he scored two goals that continue to define football almost forty years later. The first was the infamous Hand of God goal, illegal but unforgettable. The second was the Goal of the Century, a run that began inside his own half and ended with him dribbling past almost the entire England team before scoring. It remains one of the greatest individual goals the sport has ever witnessed. Argentina went on to win the World Cup, claiming their second title, and Maradona cemented his place among football's immortals.
The rivalry never really calmed down after that. There was the dramatic Round of 16 meeting in 1998, remembered as much for David Beckham's red card as for the football itself. Four years later, England defeated Argentina in the group stage of the 2002 World Cup through David Beckham's penalty, a result that contributed to Argentina's early elimination. Somehow, every chapter of this rivalry has produced controversy, unforgettable moments, or football history. Very few international fixtures can claim that.
## Another Chapter Begins Tonight
Now it is 2026, and history has brought them together once again. Argentina and England meet tonight in the World Cup semi-final. England arrive with Harry Kane, Jude Bellingham, Bukayo Saka, Anthony Gordon and a squad that has already overcome Norway led by Erling Haaland and then Mexico to reach this stage. Argentina, on the other hand, haven't looked particularly convincing throughout the tournament, but they have developed a remarkable habit of surviving. Time and again they have left it until the final ten or fifteen minutes before finding a way through. They have already escaped difficult situations more than once, and somehow they keep doing it. With Julián Álvarez, Alexis Mac Allister, Lautaro Martínez and, above all, Lionel Messi, Argentina always carry the feeling that the match is never really over until the final whistle.
This genuinely feels like one of those nights that football fans will remember for years. History is already written between these two countries. Tonight, another chapter will be added.
And like someone on the internet brilliantly said,
> **Last time England faced the Hand of God. This time, they face God himself.**
