Let me say something before anything else. Drishyam is a Mohanlal film. It always was. The Hindi remake exists, Ajay Devgn is a fine actor in his own right, but when certain PR machinery started calling Drishyam a franchise resurrected by Bollywood, it said more about the insecurity of that machinery than about the films themselves. Anyone who has followed Indian cinema with any seriousness knows where Georgekutty was born, and it was not in Hindi. That conversation can end there.
Now, Drishyam 3.
## The First Half Problem
I watched it on the very first show. And for most of the first half, I was waiting. Not in the good way, not in the way Drishyam trains you to wait, where every quiet scene feels like a fuse being lit. Just waiting. The film moved slowly without the payoff that slow movement usually promises. There was one moment around the interval, a light punch, an oh, this kind of moment, enough to keep you seated and curious. But for a franchise built entirely on the architecture of tension, the first half was remarkably flat.
## The Second Half Recovers, Mostly
The second half was better. The tension momentum that Drishyam runs on started building properly, and you could feel the film finding its feet. The familiar machinery kicked in. Stakes were raised. The knot in your stomach returned. And then came the climax, which is where the Drishyam franchise has always lived, where Georgekutty's mind finally reveals what it has been quietly constructing the whole time, and it delivered that note reasonably well.
Right up until it did not.
## The Hero Problem
Here is where Drishyam 3 loses something the first two films never lost. Georgekutty was always an ordinary man. A cable operator with a Class Four education and a fierce, almost animal love for his family. What made him extraordinary was not strength or power or heroism. It was survival instinct. It was the terrifying clarity of someone with nothing to lose except the people he loves. Drishyam and Drishyam 2 understood this completely. He was never a hero. He was a father cornered into impossible choices, and that was far more frightening and far more moving than any hero could be.
Drishyam 3 makes him a hero. Not in a subtle way. The plot bends toward it. The story stops feeling organic and starts feeling constructed around a version of Georgekutty that the first two films specifically refused to create. That is where it loses me. Not because the film becomes unwatchable, it does not. But because it becomes ordinary in the way Georgekutty never was.
## The Last Twenty Minutes
And then, just when you have made your peace with what the film is, it gives you something genuinely brilliant. The final twenty minutes shift gears in a way that earns your attention back. The last five minutes in particular landed like a proper Drishyam moment. Not a Mohanlal moment. A Georgekutty moment. That distinction matters enormously. The character, not the star, delivered the punch. And for those few minutes, the film remembered exactly what it was supposed to be.
So here is my honest summary. A below average first half, an average second half, twenty minutes of real tension, and five minutes that reminded you why this character deserved to exist in the first place. Is it a must watch for every Drishyam fan? Yes, genuinely. But that is a different thing from being a good film.
## The Sequel Nobody Needed
Drishyam 2 made sense. The story was finished yet, the wound had not closed, and Georgekutty still had something to protect. It followed the internal logic of the original without inflating it. That film earned its existence.
Drishyam 3 did not have the same necessity. The plot felt assembled rather than grown. The heroism felt imposed rather than earned. And then Jeethu Joseph placed a mid credit scene that signals a fourth film, and whatever goodwill the ending had built quietly deflated.
There is a particular kind of disappointment that comes not from a bad film but from an unnecessary one. Drishyam 3 is not bad. It is just one film too many, milking a story that had already said what it came to say, stretching a character who was most powerful precisely because he was never meant to be stretched this far. Georgekutty's greatest quality was that he was one of us. The more films you make about him, the less that becomes true.
Some cows, as they say, you milk once. Maybe twice if the season is right. The third time, you are just being greedy. By saying that, I would still get the FDFS ticket for Drishyam 4 too.
