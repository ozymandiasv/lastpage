It started with the Beatles. Not football, not Anfield, not You'll Never Walk Alone echoing through a stadium I had never seen in person. The Beatles. That is where Liverpool first entered my life, as a city, as a sound, as something that felt like it carried more weight than most things do. Football came later. And when it did, I did the thing most kids in India do. I followed the crowd. Manchester United. The trophies, the glamour, the names everyone recognised. I was that fan for a while. I am not proud of it but I am not ashamed either. It was a beginning.
Then something shifted. I am not sure I can explain it precisely. A glimpse of Kenny Dalglish. The quiet dignity of a club that talked about history differently than United did. United wore their history like armour. Liverpool carried theirs like a wound that had never fully healed. That felt more honest to me. More human. So I crossed over and I never went back.
## The Long Wait
What nobody tells you about supporting Liverpool, at least from the outside, from a country where you watch games alone at odd hours with no one around you who understands why you care this much, is how long the wait is. Brendan Rodgers gave us something to believe in. That 2013-14 season still lives in me as one of the most beautiful and devastating things I have experienced as a football fan. We were so close. And then we weren't. And then we waited some more.
I never walked alone in that wait. That is the thing about this club. The community of it, the shared grief of it, the stubborn refusal to stop believing that it was coming, held me through years of near misses and disappointments and watching United wave their twentieth title in our faces like it was a personal message.
## Then Came Klopp
Jürgen Klopp did not just change Liverpool's football. He changed the atmosphere around the club. He made Anfield feel like a living thing again. And within that revolution, surrounded by extraordinary players, one man stood slightly apart from the noise. Not the loudest. Not always the most celebrated in the wider conversation. But the one who, if you were paying attention, was doing something that Liverpool had not had in a very long time.
A star. A genuine, undeniable, world class star who chose to be here and meant it.
## What Salah Gave Us
Liverpool has always had loyal players. Players who bled for the shirt, who ran until they could not run anymore, who understood what the badge meant. Robertson was that. A warrior, a soul of this club, and his departure hurts in its own way. But loyal and special are different things. Liverpool, at least since I started watching seriously, rarely had both in the same person.
Salah was both.
He gave us the Champions League in 2019. He gave us the title in 2020, that long overdue, thirty year wait ended, the one that made grown people cry in their living rooms at two in the morning. And then there was the night we equalled United's twenty league titles. The night a particular kind of noise from Old Trafford went permanently quiet. Salah was there for all of it, not just present but central, not just scoring but defining what this era of Liverpool meant.
Was he at his best last season. No. I will not pretend otherwise. But I also will not hold that against him. Not for a single moment. Because the years before that, the goals, the records, the sheer audacity of what he did week after week in a red shirt, more than paid every debt he could ever owe this club.
## What Leaves With Him
There is a particular kind of supporter feeling that only a handful of players ever produce. The feeling that something extraordinary is happening right now, in your lifetime, while you are watching, and that you will tell people about it later. Gerrard gave me that. Klopp gave me that as a manager. Salah gave me that as a footballer, consistently, for years, in a way I had not felt since Gerrard himself.
He was not just a Liverpool player. He was the reason an entire generation of fans, scattered across the world, felt like they had a stake in something real. He was the reason I stopped feeling like an outsider supporter and started feeling like this club was genuinely mine.
Robertson leaves too. And I feel that. Truly. But this one is different and I think every honest Liverpool fan knows why. Robertson was the heartbeat. Salah was the electricity. You can find another heartbeat. The electricity, the specific kind he carried, does not come around often.
## The King Does Not Stop Being the King
He leaves. The shirt stays. The goals stay. The memories of those nights, the Champions League final, the title winning season, the records, the sheer joy of watching him run at defenders knowing something was about to happen, those do not leave with him.
Mo Salah made me fall completely in love with this football club. Not the idea of it, not the history of it, but the living breathing present tense of it. That is not a small thing. That is everything.
Long live the King.
