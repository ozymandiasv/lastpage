I've somehow cultivated a reading habit over the last few years, and when I cannot push myself through particularly difficult books, I switch to audiobooks. That is exactly what happened with War and Peace. Reading Tolstoy felt less like reading a novel and more like being dropped into a foreign country without a map. It took me nearly two months to finish, and if I am being honest, I hated it in the beginning. There were too many characters, too many names, too many aristocrats talking in drawing rooms about people I could barely keep track of. Prince Andrei Bolkonsky, Natasha Rostova, Pierre Bezukhov. The names felt foreign and slippery. They refused to stay in my head. I could not remember who was related to whom, who mattered, who would disappear, or why any of it was important. More than once I considered abandoning the book altogether. But something strange happened. The further I went, the larger the novel became. Every chapter seemed to reveal another layer beneath it. By the time I reached the end, I genuinely thought I had just finished the greatest book I have ever read.
## The Redemptions Hidden Everywhere
What struck me most about War and Peace is that it feels like a story built entirely around redemption. Not redemption in the simple sense of becoming a better person, but redemption through understanding. Almost every major character spends the novel searching for something they believe will finally complete them. Prince Andrei spends much of the story chasing glory. He wants greatness. He wants significance. He wants to become the kind of man history remembers. In many ways he is fascinated by Napoleon Bonaparte because he sees in him a reflection of what he hopes to become. Yet as the novel progresses, Tolstoy slowly dismantles that dream. Andrei's eventual death is one of the most profound things I have encountered in literature because it does not feel like an ending. It feels like a release. He arrives at a place beyond ambition, beyond resentment, beyond worldly achievement. He discovers a kind of divine acceptance that frees him from the very desires that once defined him. His death is not tragic. It is transcendent.
Pierre Bezukhov and Natasha Rostova travel a similarly difficult road. Pierre spends most of the novel stumbling from one existential crisis to another, searching for meaning through Freemasonry, philosophy, social reform, and intellectual pursuits while never quite feeling at home anywhere. Natasha, meanwhile, burns with life. She makes mistakes, suffers deeply because of them, and slowly grows into someone wiser without losing the vitality that makes her special. By the time they find each other, their union feels earned because neither of them could have become the person they needed to be without first breaking apart.
## The Lie of Glory
One of the reasons War and Peace remains relevant more than a century later is because Tolstoy understood something that many modern discussions about war still fail to grasp. War is often discussed through strategy, heroism, and national pride. Tolstoy strips all of that away. For Andrei, war begins as a stage upon which greatness can be achieved. Like countless young men throughout history, he sees it as a path toward purpose and distinction. Then reality intervenes. One of the most unforgettable moments in the novel occurs when he lies wounded on the battlefield staring into the vast sky above him. Suddenly all the glory he once sought appears absurdly small. The sky remains indifferent to victories, defeats, empires, and ambitions.
That scene stayed with me long after I finished the book because it captures Tolstoy's entire philosophy of war. There is no romance in it. There is no grandeur. There is only suffering, confusion, chance, and death. Generals imagine themselves directing history, but they are often reacting to events they barely understand. Soldiers die for plans that change before they are even executed. Historians later describe wars as though every outcome was inevitable, but Tolstoy shows how much of history is shaped by chaos. The novel repeatedly argues that human beings overestimate their control over events, and nowhere is that more obvious than on a battlefield.
## The Emperor and the Fire
Before reading War and Peace, Napoleon's invasion of Russia existed in my mind as a historical fact, a sentence found in textbooks. Tolstoy transformed it into something tangible. The burning of Moscow, the retreat of the French army, the collapse of what once seemed unstoppable, all of it unfolds with remarkable clarity. What fascinated me most was Tolstoy's portrayal of Napoleon himself. He neither worships him nor demonizes him. Instead, he reduces him to a human being.
That sounds simple, but it is remarkably difficult to do. Most historical narratives turn figures like Napoleon into myths. Tolstoy dismantles the myth. His Napoleon is a man intoxicated by his own legend, convinced of his importance, unable to recognize how little control he actually possesses. I have always admired Napoleon more for the reforms he introduced than for his military campaigns, but War and Peace made me appreciate something else entirely. It revealed the distance between the stories great men tell themselves and the reality surrounding them. The Emperor imagines himself directing history while history quietly ignores him.
## The Characters Who Endure
For all the attention given to Pierre and Andrei, the character who perhaps impressed me the most was Marya Bolkonskaya. In a novel filled with people searching for meaning, Marya already possesses it. She suffers under a tyrannical father, sacrifices her own happiness repeatedly, and endures hardships that would break many of the novel's other characters. Yet she remains compassionate without becoming naïve and strong without becoming hard. Her strength is not dramatic. It does not announce itself. It simply exists.
There is something deeply admirable about a character who does not need to discover who they are because they never lost themselves in the first place. Marya's arc feels gentle compared to the others, but that gentleness is precisely what makes it powerful. She represents a kind of quiet moral certainty that the rest of the cast spends hundreds of pages trying to find.
Oddly enough, the character I related to most was Sonya Rostova. Not because she is the most important character, but because she is one of the few who never receives the kind of grand redemption literature usually promises. Some people become heroes. Some find love. Some achieve peace. And some simply endure. Sonya belongs to that final category, so do I.
## The Beauty of Continuation
When I finished War and Peace, I expected closure. Instead, Tolstoy offered something far more unsettling and perhaps far more truthful. Life does not really conclude. It continues. Characters change, suffer, love, forgive, and die, but the world keeps moving regardless. There is no final revelation waiting at the end. No perfect resolution that ties every thread together. Some people find redemption. Some find peace. Some remain lost. Some simply keep going.
Perhaps that is why the novel affected me so deeply. It refuses to treat life as a story with a neat ending. Instead, it treats life as something ongoing, messy, and unfinished. In the end, War and Peace is not really about war or peace at all. It is about human beings spending their lives searching for meaning and occasionally discovering that meaning was never hidden in glory, achievement, or certainty. It was hidden in endurance, love, forgiveness, and the quiet acceptance that nothing truly ends.
