I want to start with something honest. I never respected Manchester City. Not really. Not in the way you respect a club that has earned something over time, that carries history in its bones, that means something beyond the money that built it. City, for most of my life as a football fan, was background noise. A club that existed in the shadow of United, in the same city, perpetually second. And then Pep Guardiola walked in and I had to rethink everything.
Not the club. The club I still feel the way I feel. But the football. What he did to that football. That I could not look away from.
## What He Did in Barcelona First
Before City, before the relentless title wins and the quadruple and the Champions League nights at the Etihad, there was Barcelona. And before Barcelona the manager, there was Barcelona the player, the one who sat at the base of the midfield and conducted everything around him with a quiet authority that most players never find in an entire career.
But it is what he built as a manager at Barca that matters here. Because that team, that specific team between 2008 and 2012, is the most beautiful football I have ever watched. Not effective. Not tactically impressive. Beautiful. The kind of football that made you feel like the game was capable of being art if the right people were in charge of it.
And at the centre of it was Messi. Which brings me to the thing I genuinely believe and will not apologise for saying. Pep Guardiola made Messi. Not invented him, not created the talent, that was there from the beginning and would have found a way regardless. But he built the system around him that allowed Messi to become something the game had never seen. He looked at this small Argentine and understood, before anyone else fully did, exactly what he was capable of if you freed him from conventional positioning and built the entire team around his movement. That false nine role, that total freedom, that was Pep's decision. And what Messi became in those years, the Ballon d'Or after Ballon d'Or, the nights that made grown footballers stop and stare, that was built on a foundation Guardiola constructed.
Xavi. Iniesta. The tiki taka that the whole world spent a decade trying to imitate and nobody ever quite managed. That was his classroom and he graduated it into something that changed how every coach on earth thinks about possession.
## The Rivalry That Made Both of Them
Then he came to City. And Klopp was already at Liverpool. And what followed was the finest managerial rivalry modern football has produced. Two men with completely different philosophies, different personalities, different ideas about what the game should look like, pushing each other to levels neither might have reached alone.
I am a Liverpool fan. I walked through the Klopp years with the specific joy of someone who had waited a long time for something like that. But I was never blind to what was happening on the other side of it. Guardiola's City were extraordinary. The football they played under him, the patience of it, the intelligence of it, the way they could suffocate a team without ever seeming to break a sweat, was something genuinely worth watching even when it was happening to your club.
Aguero scoring that goal. De Bruyne threading passes through gaps that should not have existed. The invincible season. The treble. The Champions League finally arriving after years of it being the one thing that escaped him in England. All of it built by one man's vision of what the game could be.
## The Era That Is Closing
And now here we are. Klopp left last year. Salah has just said goodbye to Anfield, and I wrote about that separately because it deserved its own space. Robertson too. An entire generation of players and managers who defined a decade of English football, walking out one by one, and Guardiola is the latest and in some ways the largest.
There is a particular feeling that comes with these farewells when they arrive close together. Not just the loss of an individual but the recognition that something larger is ending. An era. A specific quality of football, a specific level of ambition and intelligence and personality at the top of the game, that shaped everything around it for years. Klopp and Guardiola did not just manage football clubs. They managed the conversation about what football was supposed to be. Every other manager in England spent the last decade responding to what these two were doing. That is an extraordinary thing and we will only fully understand how extraordinary when we see what comes after.
## What He Leaves Behind
Pep Guardiola leaves City with more titles than any manager in their history. He leaves English football with a permanently altered understanding of how the game can be played. He leaves the next generation of coaches with an entire library of tactical innovation to study and argue about for decades.
And he leaves me, someone who never respected his club, with an admission I have no choice but to make. I respected him. Completely and without reservation. The genius of him. The relentlessness of him. The way he never stopped thinking, never settled for what had worked before, never allowed his teams to become comfortable with their own excellence.
He made Messi a God. He made City relevant in a way money alone never could have. He made Klopp better by forcing him to compete with something extraordinary. And he made football, for a sustained and remarkable period of time, feel like it was being played at a level the game had never quite reached before.
The architect has left the building. What he built stays. But there will not be another one like him. Not for a very long time.
