I came across a post today criticizing freebies and shortly afterward watched a video of Rahul Gandhi defending them. His argument was fairly straightforward. If governments can provide benefits to people like Gautam Adani and Mukesh Ambani, then there is nothing wrong with providing benefits to ordinary citizens. The statement is not entirely wrong, but it also sidesteps the real issue. One of the most frustrating things about political discussions in India is that nobody seems interested in answering the actual question. Supporters of welfare schemes defend them unconditionally. Critics oppose them unconditionally. Somewhere in between, the conversation becomes trapped inside slogans. What exactly is a freebie? Who deserves public support? When does assistance become dependency? These are the questions that matter, yet they are rarely discussed. Instead, everyone argues over labels while the underlying economics are quietly ignored.
## A Society Cannot Survive on Advice
Personally, I have never liked the word freebie. It sounds like somebody is receiving a gift. It creates the impression that people are collecting benefits they neither need nor deserve. In reality, most welfare programs exist because a significant number of people cannot meet their basic needs through the labour market alone. India may be one of the fastest-growing economies in the world, but growth statistics often conceal uncomfortable realities. Unemployment remains a serious concern, millions of people possess qualifications that the market has no meaningful use for. Millions are engaged in irregular, low-paying work. Millions of women and youth contribute through unpaid household labour that never appears in economic statistics. Labour force participation remains lower than it should be, while disguised unemployment continues to exist across large sections of the economy.
Whenever these issues are raised, someone inevitably offers the same advice. Start a business. Learn a skill. Work harder. Adapt. It sounds inspiring until it collides with reality. Economies are not self-help books. A society cannot simply motivate millions of people into employment if sufficient opportunities do not exist. People still need food, shelter, medicine, and basic dignity while they navigate those circumstances. That is where financial assistance enters the picture. Not as a luxury. Not as generosity. As survival.
## Life Support Is Not Development
The problem is that welfare is often discussed as though it were a development strategy. It is not. Giving financial assistance to struggling families will not suddenly transform the economy. It will not create industries. It will not improve productivity. It will not solve structural unemployment. Welfare is closer to life support than development. It exists because allowing people to collapse into poverty is socially and economically worse than helping them survive. A government should ideally create enough employment opportunities that fewer citizens require assistance in the first place. But until that happens, financial support becomes unavoidable. Criticizing welfare without addressing the conditions that make welfare necessary is like criticizing painkillers while ignoring the injury.
## The Subsidies Nobody Calls Freebies
What fascinates me is how selectively people use the word freebie. A poor family receiving a few thousand rupees becomes evidence of fiscal irresponsibility. A farmer receiving assistance becomes a burden on taxpayers. A student receiving support becomes proof that the government is encouraging dependency. Yet when governments provide subsidized land, tax incentives, favorable regulations, debt restructuring, or infrastructure support to large corporations, the language changes immediately. Suddenly it is not a freebie. Suddenly it is an investment.
To be fair, there is logic behind that argument. Industrial growth creates jobs. Factories create economic activity. Infrastructure creates opportunity. Every successful economy has used some form of industrial policy to accelerate development. The problem arises when the returns fail to justify the support. If public resources are being directed toward private business, then the public deserves something meaningful in return. Jobs. Productivity. Opportunity. Economic mobility.
This is where the discussion becomes uncomfortable.
## The Return Nobody Questions
Take Gautam Adani as an example. At one point, even figures associated with the Adani Group suggested a workforce measured in 20-30k. For a business empire worth tens of billions of dollars, that number feels surprisingly small. The exact figure is less important than the larger question it raises. If public support for corporations is justified because it creates employment and broad economic benefits, how much support is reasonable when wealth grows exponentially while job creation grows far more slowly?
Imagine a businessman accumulating wealth at extraordinary speed while employing a workforce that would barely fill a medium-sized town. At what point does society stop calling that an investment and start asking whether the returns justify the cost? We scrutinize every rupee spent on the poor. We rarely apply the same scrutiny to benefits flowing upward.
## The Taxpayer Who Loves Subsidies
There is another contradiction I cannot stop thinking about. The average salaried professional spends an impressive amount of time trying to reduce taxes. Investments are carefully planned. Deductions are maximized. Exemptions are claimed. Every legal loophole is explored. There is nothing wrong with this. Almost everybody would do the same. Given the opportunity, most people prefer keeping more of their own money.
What fascinates me is what happens next.
The same individual who proudly saves fifty thousand, one lakh, or even several lakhs through tax planning suddenly becomes furious when somebody else receives a few thousand rupees to cover basic living expenses. Suddenly welfare becomes reckless. Suddenly public spending becomes irresponsible. Suddenly dependency becomes a moral concern.
The contradiction is difficult to ignore. One person’s financial benefit is described as smart financial planning. Another person’s financial benefit is described as a freebie. The difference often has less to do with economics and more to do with who receives the money.
## The Real Failure
The real failure is not welfare. The real failure is creating a society where welfare becomes permanently necessary. A healthy economy should not force millions of people to depend on government assistance for survival. But it should not force them to suffer while waiting for economic growth to eventually reach them either. Likewise, support for corporations should not be judged by the size of the companies receiving it but by the public value created in return.
Perhaps that is why the entire freebie debate feels dishonest. The question is not whether money goes to the poor or the rich. The question is whether public resources are solving public problems. Everything else is branding. We call assistance to struggling citizens a burden. We call assistance to powerful corporations an investment. Then we act surprised when nobody can agree on what a freebie actually is.
