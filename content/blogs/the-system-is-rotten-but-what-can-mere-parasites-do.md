I want to start with a word. One word, chosen by the highest judicial authority in the country, spoken from a bench that is supposed to be the last refuge of the ordinary citizen in this republic.
> Cockroaches.
That is what Chief Justice of India Suryakant called youngsters who turn to social media, RTI activism, and journalism when the profession has no place for them. "There are youngsters like cockroaches who are not getting employment in the profession. Some are on social media, some become RTI activists," he said. This was not a slip. This was the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court of India, on the record, in open court, describing citizens exercising their democratic rights as parasites on society.
## The Clarification That Made It Worse
Yesterday he clarified. He said his comments were aimed only at those entering professions with fake degrees, not at the youth broadly. "What I had specifically criticised were those who have entered professions like the Bar with the aid of fake and bogus degrees," he said, adding that he sees the youth as pillars of a developed India. 
I want to take that clarification seriously. I genuinely do. But it does not hold.
Because here is the thing about RTI activism, which he specifically named. The Right to Information Act does not require a degree. Not a real one, not a fake one, not any one at all. Any citizen of India, regardless of qualification, employment status, or educational background, has the full legal right to file an RTI. That is not a loophole. That is the entire point of the law. It was designed precisely so that the ordinary person, the one without institutional access, without connections, without a black robe, could ask the state to account for itself.
So even if we accept his clarification completely, even if every single person he was picturing had a fake degree, they still had every legal right to do exactly what he was criticizing them for doing. The clarification does not rescue the statement. It just makes the contempt for citizen participation more specific.
## What Happens When You Question the System
Sometime last month, Arvind Kejriwal appeared before Justice Swarana Kanta Sharma in the Delhi excise policy case and sought her recusal, alleging bias. His team had circulated a video alleging she was speaking at an RSS event. The judge clarified the video was edited and that she was in fact speaking at a college event. Justice Sharma initiated contempt proceedings against Kejriwal and several AAP leaders for what she described as defamatory and vilifying allegations against her and the court, after which she recused herself from the case. A PIL was then filed seeking removal of all videos of the recusal hearing from the internet, and the court directed Google and X to take them down.
I am not saying Kejriwal was right. The edited video was irresponsible. But the pattern is worth noticing. A citizen questions a judge publicly. The institution responds with contempt proceedings and an internet takedown order. That is how accountability flows in this system, strictly in one direction.
## The Textbook and the Cash
A Class 8 NCERT chapter discussing judicial pendency and corruption sparked a major controversy. The Supreme Court took suo motu cognizance, leading NCERT to apologize and withdraw the content. CJI Suryakant called it a product of a deep rooted conspiracy. The chapter contained factual data about vacancies, pending cases, and undertrial prisoners. Children were not allowed to read numbers because numbers, apparently, defame the judiciary. 
And then there is Justice Yashwant Varma. Firefighters responding to a blaze at his official Delhi residence found sacks of partially burnt five hundred rupee notes in a locked storeroom. A three judge inquiry panel found strong inferential evidence that the cash was under the covert or active control of Justice Varma and his family and recommended impeachment. Justice Varma challenged the process in the Supreme Court. The Supreme Court dismissed his challenge, clearing the path for Parliament to initiate impeachment proceedings. No FIR has been filed. No criminal investigation opened. Parliament has not moved.
## What Mere Parasites Can Do
The system is not broken. A broken system is one that failed to work. This one works exactly as designed. It protects those inside it. It removes textbooks that embarrass it. It takes down videos that question it. It calls citizens cockroaches and then clarifies that it only meant the ones with fake degrees, as if that makes it better, as if the RTI does not belong to every single one of them regardless.
The CJI asked Sanjay Dubey whether he wanted to join the parasites. I keep thinking the parasites are the only ones still asking questions the institution would rather not answer.
And maybe that is exactly why it needed a word for them.
