The protest by Indian students took an entirely different turn yesterday, perhaps becoming one of the biggest student-led movements India has witnessed in the last decade. But what struck me wasn't its size. It was how different it felt. India has traditionally been a country where large-scale protests, despite their intensity, have largely remained peaceful. I was in my early 20s when Anna Hazare led the anti-corruption movement against the UPA government. Back then, I genuinely thought Lokpal was some magical solution that would simply eliminate corruption overnight. Little did I understand the political motivations, the narratives, and the power struggles surrounding that movement. Since then, Indian politics has changed dramatically. A new government came to power, narratives became stronger than policies, political fandom grew stronger than political debate, and questioning those in power became increasingly difficult. Environmental activists protested. Farmers protested. Many others protested. Whether one agreed with them or not almost stopped mattering because their voices rarely seemed to receive the attention they deserved. But something changed yesterday. Repeated examination paper leaks, allegations of corruption, and growing frustration among students had already created anger. Then came the remarks by the Chief Justice referring to students and PIL petitioners as "cockroaches." Suddenly, what began as outrage over examinations transformed into something much larger. India witnessed what many online have now started calling the "Cockroach Movement."
What I don't remember seeing very often is a peaceful student movement being met with the kind of force that appeared in many of the videos circulating yesterday. Video after video showed students alleging they were lathi-charged, tear gas being used, and protesters being physically dispersed during their march towards Parliament. What disturbed me even more were videos that appeared to show individuals, whom many protesters alleged were neither police officers nor identifiable security personnel, assaulting students. I deliberately say "alleged" because that distinction matters. But from the footage that has emerged so far, many of those individuals certainly did not appear to be in uniform or easily identifiable as law enforcement. Unlike earlier movements, this protest belongs to a generation that carries a camera in its pocket. Even if television channels choose not to show what happened, the protesters themselves can. Every confrontation, every allegation, every clash, every moment is recorded and uploaded within minutes. That changes everything.
There were also allegations that some individuals vandalised property while pretending to be students in an attempt to portray the movement as violent. Whether those allegations ultimately prove true is for investigations to determine, but one thing is certain. If the overwhelming majority of students are protesting peacefully, then that is the movement the government should engage with. It should answer them. It should listen to them. Democracies are not weakened because students ask difficult questions. They are weakened when governments begin believing they no longer need to answer them. The longer genuine grievances are ignored, the larger they become, and the harder they become to resolve.
If peaceful protests continue to go unheard and are met primarily with force instead of dialogue, frustration will only continue to grow. History has repeatedly shown that governments rarely face their greatest challenge from the first protest. They face it after years of refusing to listen. But responsibility does not rest with the government alone. Moments like these also demand leadership from the opposition. Major opposition leaders should be standing with students, raising their concerns in Parliament, and ensuring that their voices are heard, regardless of political affiliation. As of now, Rahul Gandhi has not directly involved himself in this movement, and that silence is just as noticeable. Democracies work best when both the government and the opposition treat public grievances as matters that deserve attention, not as opportunities to remain silent. I sincerely hope India never reaches a point where young people begin believing that nobody in power is willing to listen.
