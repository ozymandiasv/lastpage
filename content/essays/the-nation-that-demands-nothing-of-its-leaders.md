B.R. Ambedkar warned us at the very founding of this republic, "Democracy in India, is only a top-dressing on an Indian soil which is essentially undemocratic." He said it in 1949. We have spent seven decades proving him right.
I live in a tier one city. Not a village, not a small town. A city with infrastructure, colleges, hospitals, a functioning middle class. My Member of Parliament holds a graduate degree from a politically connected family. That is the ceiling. My MLA is eighth pass. Not a typo. Eighth standard. And surrounding him, his sons, his local body associates, a constellation of dynasty masquerading as governance. Nobody asked what he knows. Nobody needed to. He was born into the right family. That was credential enough.
## The Inverted Hierarchy
Here is the thing that I cannot get out of my head. To become a peon in a government office, you need to have cleared your twelfth standard. That is the bare minimum to file papers, to stamp documents, to sit at a desk and follow instructions from people who matter. And I want to be clear, there is dignity in that work. All work has dignity. But the system itself has decided that this is where the floor sits. Twelfth pass, or you do not qualify.
The people those peons answer to? No such floor exists. An MLA can be eighth pass. A Union Minister can have qualifications that do not hold up under scrutiny. The Prime Minister's educational record has been the subject of more RTI applications and court cases than most citizens can keep track of. We have built a country where accountability increases precisely as power decreases. The less you matter, the more you must prove. The more power you hold, the less anyone is allowed to ask.
Ambedkar also said something that cuts even deeper here. "However good a Constitution may be, if those who are implementing it are not good, it will prove to be bad." We have handed the Constitution to people we never stopped to qualify. And then we act surprised when it bends.
## What We Actually Vote For
People voted for my MLA because of his family name. Because his father held the seat before him. Because caste math worked in his favour. Because someone in the village remembered a favour done two elections ago. Because the money came at the right time. These are not cynical observations. These are documented, practised, openly understood realities of how elections function in large parts of this country.
And here is the uncomfortable part. Every one of those votes counted the same as mine. Same weight. Same value. One person, one vote. That is the procedural promise of democracy, and it is not wrong on its face. But we have mistaken the ritual for the substance. We have confused the act of voting with the existence of genuine representation. A vote cast under coercion, a vote sold for a thousand rupees, a vote placed out of pure caste loyalty with no knowledge of any policy, these carry the same democratic weight as a vote made after reading, thinking, comparing, and choosing. The math is equal. The meaning is not.
I know how this sounds. I know the counterargument arrives fast. Any system that qualifies votes becomes a system that excludes, and exclusion has always fallen hardest on those already excluded. That is a real concern. It deserves to be taken seriously. But taking it seriously does not mean pretending the current system is working. It means sitting with the tension honestly.
## The Reckoning That Never Comes
What I want, at minimum, is accountability with teeth. Not performance reviews, not press conferences, not opposition outrage that evaporates after the news cycle. Real consequences. An independent body with the sole mandate of documenting what was promised, what was delivered, and what was looted. Not a body controlled by the ruling party or the opposition, not one staffed by appointments made by the very people it is meant to examine. Something structurally separate, with the power to inform voters before every election about the actual record of the person asking for their vote again.
Right now there is no reckoning. A politician can fail completely, loot openly, lie systematically, and file nomination papers for the next election on the same promises he broke in the last one. And win. Because the machinery that should create accountability has been thoroughly captured by the people it was meant to hold.
Bureaucracy could theoretically be a counterweight. In theory, a strong civil service insulates governance from the worst political instincts. In practice, the bureaucracy is not insulated. It is compliant. Transfers, postings, promotions, all of it flows through political will. The counterweight has been absorbed.
## The Part That Is Hardest to Say
The system is broken. That much is easy to write. The harder thing is the line that follows it. Ambedkar stood before the Constituent Assembly and asked how long we would continue living a life of contradictions, recognising one person one vote in politics while denying equality in our social and economic life, and warned that those who suffer from inequality will eventually blow up the structure of political democracy itself. That was 1949. The contradictions have not resolved. They have compounded.
We keep participating in this system, and then register shock when it returns exactly what we put into it. The dynasty candidate wins because we vote for the dynasty. The eighth pass MLA governs because we handed him the mandate. The promises get broken because there is no cost for breaking them, and we made sure of that by returning the same faces.
I do not have a clean solution. I genuinely do not. But I know that the first step is refusing to look away from what is actually happening. Not wrapping it in the flag, not soothing it with nationalism, not explaining it away with whataboutery about other countries. Just looking at it directly.
We deserve better governance. But more than that, we need to stop making it so easy for bad governance to survive.
