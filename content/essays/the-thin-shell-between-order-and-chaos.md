Dostoevsky said that a beast can never be as cruel as a human being, so artistically, so picturesquely cruel. He was not being dramatic. He was being honest in a way most people are not comfortable with. Because if you look at history long enough, not the curated version they teach in schools but the actual record of what humans do to each other the moment the rules stop applying, you cannot argue with him.
That is the thing nobody wants to say out loud. We are not civilised by nature. We are civilised by arrangement. And arrangements break.
## What Leadership Actually Is
We talk about leadership like it is about vision and charisma and the right speech at the right moment. It is not. At its most fundamental, leadership is the management of distance. The distance between order and the void that sits just beneath it. When leaders remember that, when they feel the weight of what they are holding together, things hold. When they forget, when power stops feeling like responsibility and starts feeling like reward, the distance closes. And it closes faster than anyone expects.
Corruption is never a sudden event. It is a slow drift. A series of small decisions that individually seem manageable and collectively become catastrophic. And the drift is always the same direction. Away from the people. Toward the self. History has not produced a single exception to this pattern that I can find.
## The Guillotine Does Not Ask Whose Side You Were On
The French Revolution is the image everyone reaches for and the one nobody sits with long enough. A monarchy so rotted from within that it could no longer carry the weight of the country beneath it. When it fell, the people who had suffered under it did not build something better. They built the Terror. The guillotine fell on aristocrats and then on revolutionaries and then on anyone who looked like a threat to whoever was currently holding power. What promised equality delivered industrialised execution. What looked like liberation became its own kind of tyranny, just louder and more righteous about it.
This is not an argument for monarchy. It is an observation about what happens when structures dissolve faster than new ones can be built. The vacuum does not wait politely. It fills immediately, and it fills with the worst available option.
Bangladesh after Sheikh Hasina fled. Libya after Gaddafi. Iraq after Saddam. Every time, the same thing. People who had suffered genuine and documented oppression turned within hours into something that looked remarkably like what had oppressed them. Homes stormed. People humiliated publicly, as performance, as theatre. The fallen degraded in ways that had nothing to do with justice and everything to do with the intoxication of suddenly having power over someone who used to have power over you.
We like to think we are past this. That we have evolved. But strip the structure away and the evolution goes with it, maybe not in all of us. But in enough. Always in enough.
## The Fear Nobody Admits Works
One of the quietest things holding societies together throughout history has been religion. Not faith necessarily, but fear. The fear of something watching when the state has stopped watching. Something that remembers when the law has dissolved.
When medieval European kingdoms collapsed under warlords, the Church remained. When governments failed across parts of the Islamic world, religious moral codes held communities from complete disintegration. The fear of divine judgment, karma, hell, retribution in whatever shape a culture imagines it, has kept people from doing things that law alone never could. Because law requires enforcement. It requires someone to catch you. Fear of something omniscient requires nothing except the belief itself.
I am not saying religion is the answer. I am saying the hesitation it creates matters. That pause between impulse and action, when a person believes something is watching even in the dark, has historically been the difference between a community and a mob. We do not talk about this enough because it makes secular people uncomfortable and religious people smug. But it is true regardless of how it makes anyone feel.
## Democracy Is Flawed and It Is Still the Best We Have
Every system of governance is an attempt to solve the same problem. How do you stop power from eating itself and everyone beneath it. The honest answer is that you cannot fully solve it. You can only slow the corruption down and build mechanisms to catch it before it becomes irreversible.
Idi Amin. Ferdinand Marcos. Men who turned entire nations into personal estates while the people beneath them starved and disappeared. This is not unique to dictators. It is just more visible in systems without accountability. Democracy is slow and noisy and endlessly frustrating and frequently captured by the very interests it was designed to check. But it has one thing no other system has managed to build. The ability to remove a leader without burning the country down. The ability to say this is not working and mean it through a ballot rather than a blade. In the entire history of human governance that is a genuinely extraordinary thing. We have grown so accustomed to it that we have stopped understanding what its absence looks like. We will remember, if we ever lose it.
## The Rhyme
Rome fell. Centuries of fragmentation followed before anything resembling order returned. The Russian Revolution removed a Tsar and produced Stalin. The Arab Spring felt, for one brief and electric moment, like something new, and then became civil wars and military coups and authoritarian restorations that in several cases were worse than what preceded them.
The arc is always the same. Legitimate grievance. Revolt. Collapse in the absence of governance. A new order rising from the rubble, usually carrying the same rot the old one died from, just wearing different clothes.
History does not repeat. But it rhymes. And the rhyme, if you have been paying attention, is always corruption, revolt, chaos, order. Corruption, revolt, chaos, order. The names change. The flags change. The speeches change. The structure does not.
We are somewhere in that cycle right now. The corruption is not hidden. The frustration is not quiet. The revolts are beginning, in different places, under different banners, with different languages for the same rage. What comes after depends entirely on whether the people revolting have thought past the moment of collapse. Because history suggests most of them have not. And history is not kind to the ones who haven't.
The shell is thinner than it looks. It has always been thinner than it looks. The only thing that has ever kept it intact is the collective decision, made every single day, by leaders who remember they serve and people who remember they have power, to keep holding it together.
The moment either side stops making that decision, you already know what comes next. We have seen it before. We will see it again. The only question worth asking is whether we are paying enough attention to know which part of the cycle we are standing in right now.
