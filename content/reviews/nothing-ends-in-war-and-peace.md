Nothing Ends in War and Peace
Nothing Ends in War and Peace

Tolstoy wrote a novel about war, history, love, death, and redemption, only to arrive at the unsettling possibility that life never really resolves at all.

I’ve somehow managed to cultivate a reading habit over the last few years. Whenever I find myself struggling through particularly difficult books, I usually switch to the audiobook instead. That’s exactly what happened with War and Peace.
Reading Tolstoy didn’t feel like reading a novel. It felt like someone had dropped me into a foreign country without a map and simply expected me to figure things out. It took me almost two months to finish, and if I’m being honest, I hated it at first. There were far too many characters, far too many names, and far too many aristocrats sitting in drawing rooms discussing people I could barely remember. Prince Andrei Bolkonsky. Natasha Rostova. Pierre Bezukhov. Every few chapters I found myself wondering, “Wait, who is this again?” I couldn’t remember who belonged to which family, who mattered, who would disappear halfway through the story, or why I was supposed to care.
More than once I seriously considered putting the book down and never coming back to it. It felt exhausting rather than enjoyable. But somewhere along the way, something quietly changed. I can’t even point to the exact chapter where it happened. The novel simply became bigger. Every time I thought I understood what Tolstoy was trying to do, another layer revealed itself underneath. Characters I had ignored suddenly became fascinating. Conversations that had once felt pointless started carrying ideas that stayed with me long after I had finished reading them.
By the time I reached the final page, I genuinely felt like I had just finished the greatest novel I have ever read.
## The Redemptions Hidden Everywhere
What stayed with me the most after finishing War and Peace wasn't the war. It wasn't Napoleon either. It was how almost every major character spends the entire novel searching for something they believe will finally make their life complete, only to discover that what they were chasing was never really the answer.
Prince Andrei spends much of the novel chasing glory. He wants greatness. He wants to become the kind of man history remembers. In many ways, Napoleon becomes the embodiment of that dream. Andrei looks at him and sees everything he wishes to become. But Tolstoy has no interest in rewarding that ambition. Slowly, almost mercilessly, he strips it away. By the time Andrei reaches the end of his life, he is no longer the man who wanted history to remember his name. In fact, he almost becomes the opposite. His death never felt tragic to me. It felt strangely peaceful. Almost like he had finally understood something that the rest of the world was still too busy chasing. He lets go of ambition, resentment, and even the fear of death itself. It isn't redemption because he became a better man. It is redemption because he finally understood what actually mattered.
Pierre's journey is different but somehow arrives at the same place. He spends most of the novel wandering from one idea to another, convinced that meaning must exist somewhere if only he can find the right philosophy, the right people, or the right purpose. He throws himself into Freemasonry, social reform, intellectual debates, and almost everything else that promises answers. Yet nothing really changes him until life itself begins to humble him. Natasha's journey feels different again. She is impulsive, emotional, and painfully human. She makes mistakes that almost destroy her, suffers because of them, and somehow comes out wiser without losing the warmth that made her special in the first place. By the time Pierre and Natasha finally find each other, it doesn't feel like a romantic ending that Tolstoy had planned all along. It feels like two people who had to completely lose themselves before they could truly understand one another.
## The Lie of Glory
One of the reasons War and Peace still feels relevant today is because Tolstoy understood something that we still struggle to accept. We love talking about war through victories, strategies, famous generals, and heroic charges. History books often make wars feel inevitable, almost glorious. Tolstoy tears all of that apart.
For Andrei, war begins as an opportunity. Like countless young men before and after him, he believes it is where greatness is earned. Then comes the moment that stayed with me more than anything else in the novel. He lies wounded on the battlefield and looks up at the sky. It is such a simple scene, yet it completely changes the novel. Suddenly every ambition that once consumed him seems absurdly small. The sky doesn't care who wins the battle. It doesn't care about Napoleon, Russia, or glory. It simply exists. I kept thinking about that scene long after I finished the book because it quietly destroys almost every romantic idea people have about war.
That, I think, is Tolstoy's greatest achievement. He doesn't simply tell you that war is terrible. Plenty of writers have done that. He makes you realise how insignificant the things people kill and die for often become when viewed from a little distance. Generals believe they are directing history, yet they spend much of their time reacting to events they barely understand. Soldiers die for plans that change before they are ever carried out. Historians later explain battles as though everything happened exactly as it was supposed to, but Tolstoy constantly reminds us how much of history is shaped by chance, confusion, and ordinary human error. If there is one argument the novel keeps making over and over again, it is that human beings greatly overestimate how much control they really have.
## The Emperor and the Fire
Before reading War and Peace, Napoleon's invasion of Russia existed in my mind as little more than a chapter from a history textbook. I knew the broad outline. Napoleon invaded. Moscow burned. Winter arrived. The French retreated. But history has a strange way of making enormous events feel distant. Tolstoy somehow makes them feel immediate. By the time I reached the sections describing the burning of Moscow and the retreat of the French army, I no longer felt like I was reading history. I felt like I was watching an empire slowly collapse under the weight of its own certainty.
What fascinated me most, though, was Tolstoy's portrayal of Napoleon himself. He neither worships him nor tries to turn him into a villain. Instead, he does something far more interesting. He treats him as a man. That sounds obvious, but most historical writing has a habit of turning people like Napoleon into myths. Tolstoy quietly dismantles that myth. His Napoleon is convinced that he is directing history, making decisions that shape the world. Yet chapter after chapter, you begin to realise that history is unfolding almost independently of him. He believes he is in control, while Tolstoy keeps reminding the reader that no single individual, no matter how extraordinary, has as much power over history as they imagine.
I've always admired Napoleon more for the reforms he introduced than for his military campaigns. War and Peace didn't really change that opinion. What it changed was how I think about greatness itself. The novel repeatedly asks whether great men truly shape history or whether history simply chooses certain men to stand at the front while millions of invisible lives carry everything else forward. By the end, I wasn't entirely sure Tolstoy believed in great men at all, and I think that's exactly the question he wanted his readers to leave with.
## The Characters Who Endure
For all the attention Pierre and Andrei receive, the character who perhaps stayed with me the most was Marya Bolkonskaya. In a novel where almost everyone is searching for meaning, Marya seems to have found it long before the story even begins. She spends her life under the shadow of a difficult father, repeatedly sacrifices her own happiness, and endures hardships that would have broken many of the novel's other characters. Yet she never becomes bitter. She remains compassionate without becoming naïve and strong without ever needing to prove it. There is something deeply admirable about that kind of quiet strength. It doesn't announce itself. It doesn't demand attention. It simply exists. By the end of the novel, I realised that while everyone else was trying to become someone, Marya had quietly remained herself all along.
Oddly enough, the character I related to the most was Sonya Rostova. Not because she is the most important character, but perhaps because she isn't. Literature often promises its characters redemption. They find love, purpose, success, or at the very least some grand revelation that justifies everything they have suffered through. Sonya never really receives that. She loves, she sacrifices, she endures, and life simply moves on. There is something painfully real about that. Not everyone becomes a hero. Not everyone gets the ending they deserve. Some people simply carry on, and I think Tolstoy understood that better than most writers ever have. In many ways, Sonya's story felt more human to me than some of the novel's larger triumphs.
## The Beauty of Continuation
When I finally finished War and Peace, I expected the kind of ending that most great novels promise. Some final revelation. Some perfect conclusion that would tie together everything Tolstoy had spent more than a thousand pages building. Instead, he gave me something far more unsettling. Life simply continued.
That might sound disappointing, but by the time I closed the book, I realised it was probably the most honest ending he could have written. People change. They fall in love. They lose people they thought they couldn't live without. Some discover peace. Others never do. Some dreams come true, while others quietly disappear. Yet the world keeps moving forward without ever stopping to give anyone a proper conclusion. Life doesn't pause because a story has ended.
I think that's what affected me the most. War and Peace isn't really about war, nor is it really about peace. Those are only the backdrop. At its heart, it is a novel about people trying to understand why they are here and what kind of life is worth living. Some look for the answer in glory. Others in power, wealth, religion, love, or history itself. Tolstoy patiently strips each of those away until all that remains are ordinary people trying to become a little kinder, a little wiser, and a little more accepting of the things they cannot control.
When I first picked up War and Peace, I thought I was reading one of the greatest historical novels ever written. By the time I finished it, I realised I had read something much larger than that. It is a novel about history, certainly, but it is also about family, love, ambition, faith, grief, forgiveness, and the strange ways people spend their entire lives searching for meaning. More than anything else, it is a novel that refuses to pretend life has neat endings. It reminds us that even after our greatest victories and our deepest losses, tomorrow still arrives.
Perhaps that's why War and Peace has stayed with me long after I turned the final page. It doesn't leave you with answers. It leaves you with the unsettling thought that life never really resolves itself. It simply asks us to keep living, to keep loving, and to keep going.
Maybe that's all any of us are ever doing.
