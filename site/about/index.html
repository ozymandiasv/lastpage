About

I write. Not because someone asked me to, not because there is a career in it, but because I look around and I cannot stay quiet. I watch, I read, I think, and then something in me refuses to let it pass without record. And this is only for that record. I don't need an audience.

I am a student of literature. I was a lecturer, once. What undid that, whether it was the weight of my own failures, the blade of my arrogance, or simply the way certain pasts refuse to stay buried, I do not know. Some reckonings are private. What survived all of it, intact and indifferent to everything that fell apart, were the books. Kafka, Dostoevsky, Camus, Poe. They did not abandon me when everything else did. They have been more faithful than most people I have known.

By every conventional measure, I am a failure. I do not contest that. But I have come to understand that conventional measures were never built for people who use pain as a tool. All I need is a book, a laptop, a pen, and enough to eat. That is not poverty of spirit. That is the most honest accounting of what a life actually requires.

I am a Communist. Not as a posture, not as an aesthetic borrowed from a bookshelf or a protest poster. A Communist by conviction, by conscience, by a rage that has never cooled. I look at a world where the man painting the hoarding sixty feet in the air is told he took no risk, and the man who signed the cheque is celebrated as the courageous one, and I find that arrangement not merely unjust but obscene. I do not carry a flag. I carry a grief. I want a world that means something, not this well-decorated slavery we have collectively agreed to call freedom.

I believe in the ones the world broke while they were building something for it. Jesus, not the God, the man. Read him properly. A radical who spoke of the meek inheriting the earth and was crucified by the powerful for meaning every word of it. John Lennon, who imagined a world without borders and was shot for the audacity of saying it out loud. Karl Marx, who named the machinery before most people admitted it existed. Buddha, who walked away from a palace to understand suffering and returned with something empires have never forgiven him for. Tesla, a mind so far ahead of its time that the time itself made sure he paid for it. The fallen ones. The forgotten ones. The ones who were broken lifting something heavy so others could walk lighter.

And then there is Keats. I do not place him in a list. He deserves a line of his own. A man who wrote some of the most luminous poetry the English language has ever produced and was dead at twenty five. There are people whose absence the world has never fully accounted for. Keats is one of them.

I also believe in the fiction of the lost. The ones who never existed and yet taught me more than most who did. Batman, who had the power to rule and chose instead to bleed for a city that never fully understood him. Kakashi, who carried grief like a lifelong vow and showed up anyway, every single time. Karna, perhaps the greatest tragedy ever written, a man of unmatched honour and ability, born on the wrong side of fate's preference, loyal to his last breath to people who never deserved it. Prometheus, who stole fire from the gods and gave it to humanity, and was chained to a rock for eternity as his reward. Courage does not check its source before it teaches you something. I have learned from all of them.

I am a Liverpool fan. I walk alone. I have always known I do not.

I have largely left social media behind. I surface on X and Reddit when the world demands to be witnessed, but the rest, the reels, the shorts, the infinite scroll engineered to dissolve attention into nothing, I have no use for any of it. My phone is an afterthought. My laptop is my second heart.

I imagine the world Lennon dreamed. I sit sometimes in the Octopus' Garden. I read. I write. I watch football. I watch history rhyme with itself and wonder if anyone else is listening.

That is me. Welcome, if you are here.

Why Last Page?

Think about the last page of a book. Not the last chapter, not the epilogue. The very last page. The one you turn to hoping there is something more, one more line, one more sentence, and there isn't. Or sometimes there is, and that surprises you in a way nothing else quite can. That page is where everything lands. The grief, the joy, the relief, the particular emptiness that only a finished book leaves behind. You have carried the whole weight of it to this point and now it is over and you simply sit with what remains.

That is what this place is. Everything here is a last page of something. A thought that had to end somewhere. An essay that ran out of road. A feeling that needed to be written down before it disappeared into the noise. You are either happy or devastated at a last page, rarely anywhere in between. I have always respected that honesty. I wanted to live inside it.

Why Ozymandias IV?

Two reasons, and they collapse into each other perfectly.

The first is Shelley. "My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings. Look on my works, ye Mighty, and despair." And around him, nothing. Just sand and silence and the slow indifference of time. The man raised monuments to his own permanence and the desert consumed every one of them. That poem found me young and has never left. The arrogance of permanence. The quiet joke the world plays on everyone who believes they will last. Every word I write understands it may end up in that same desert. I write anyway. Perhaps that is the only genuinely honest thing left to do.

The second is Breaking Bad. If you have seen the episode, you already know. Walter White standing in the desert, that same poem, that same ruin surrounding him. A man who mistook his own legend for something real and watched it decompose. There is a truth in that image I keep returning to, because it is not just his story. Greatness is more fragile than it pretends. Ego is sand.

As for the IV, the explanation is almost embarrassingly plain. When I went to register my gaming alias, Ozymandias was taken. So was Ozymandias I, II, and III. I arrived at IV, rolled my eyes at the indignity of it, and kept it anyway. Sometimes the most accidental things are the ones that fit. I am Ozymandiasiv now. Online, O IV. A king who could not even claim his own name on the first attempt. The desert, I think, finds this appropriate.

I live in Pune. I read more than I do most things. If something here reaches you, leave a comment or find me on X or Reddit.
