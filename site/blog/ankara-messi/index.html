Ankara Messi

From despair to belief in thirteen unforgettable minutes.

It has been quite some time since I wrote anything. I have not been religiously following this FIFA World Cup as I used to. Oh, I am, but not as much as I used to. I watch all of Argentina's matches, no matter what, and a couple of other matches, that's it. But yesterday I had to. Argentina was facing Egypt. Imagine the Egyptian King against the GOAT himself. The last time I saw them face each other was in 2019, when Liverpool obliterated Barcelona in the second leg, and that day I was supporting the other side. I was supporting Liverpool. But that wasn't the case tonight. I have never in my life supported any other country other than Argentina in a FIFA World Cup or even in friendly matches. I have supported Argentina since the 2010 World Cup. I was ready to sacrifice everything I had to see Messi win it in 2014. I was broken in 2018, and I was the happiest I had ever been in 2022. And perhaps I will be again.

From Despair to Ankara Messi

So the match began, and Egypt scored two goals. Argentina trailed at half-time in a World Cup for the first time since 2010. After 16 years, it was happening again. It was almost the 80th minute. I had lost hope. I didn't think Argentina would win this time, but I hoped they would at least draw. A meme I saw later after the match was the most apt thing to write here. The substitution board was raised. Number 10 was substituted for Number 19, the original 19, the young dangerous Messi, and that was exactly what happened. Messi started to play like a man possessed. The assist, then that magnificent goal, and finally Enzo's stoppage-time winner sealed a remarkable 3–2 comeback, sending Argentina into the quarter-finals.

Was FIFA Wrong?

Now, the game also raised a lot of questions about favouritism towards Argentina by FIFA. The decision to disallow Zizo's goal was something I initially felt was wrong until I later learned that such fouls, even those committed much earlier in the attacking move, can be reviewed under VAR's Attacking Possession Phase (APP) protocol if they directly lead to a goal. The challenge on Lisandro Martínez could also reasonably be interpreted as DOGSO (Denying an Obvious Goal-Scoring Opportunity). Initially, I didn't think the first decision should have stood because, as far as I knew, it seemed unprecedented. But yes, it is in the rule book. I am not talking about the incident involving Salah, unlike many who claim it should have resulted in a penalty. Most people making that claim either do not understand how penalties and fouls are interpreted under the Laws of the Game, or they are simply looking for evidence to support the narrative that this World Cup is rigged because the player they backed was recently eliminated. Once Julián Álvarez won possession of the ball, the remaining contact was judged as normal football contact rather than a foul. That is why no penalty was awarded. It may have looked controversial in real time, but it was consistent with how the Laws of the Game were applied in this situation. Before calling it corruption or bias, it is worth understanding how these laws actually work.

The Bigger Question

But then arises a bigger question. The first disallowed goal, like I mentioned, was within the rules, yet it also felt unprecedented. I have been watching football for quite a long time, and I have never encountered such a decision. That brings me to a very important point. Whether intentionally or not, FIFA benefits enormously from having Messi remain in the tournament. A final without Messi simply would not attract the same level of global attention. The 2022 World Cup final drew around 1.5 billion viewers worldwide. Since then, Messi has significantly accelerated football's popularity in the United States through his move to Inter Miami, making the 2026 World Cup an even bigger commercial opportunity. It is difficult not to feel that every major decision involving Argentina will be examined through that lens. Is that fair? Perhaps not. Does it prove bias? No. The decisions made in this match were supported by the Laws of the Game. But perception matters, and when unprecedented decisions consistently favour the tournament's biggest star, questions are naturally going to be asked.
