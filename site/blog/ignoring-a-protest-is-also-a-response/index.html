Ignoring a Protest Is Also a Response

Democracies are not judged by whether they agree with a protest, but by whether they are willing to answer it.

It doesn’t really matter whether you support Sonam Wangchuk’s movement against the government over the examination paper leaks or not. You can have your own perspective, your own politics, and your own reasons for agreeing or disagreeing with him. Every one of us is entitled to that. I am not saying Wangchuk is right. I am saying he deserves to be acknowledged and answered. That, to me, is the entire point of protest. Hunger strikes, satyagraha, peaceful demonstrations—they are not supposed to guarantee that the government agrees with you. They are supposed to force those in power to respond. That is why Gandhi’s satyagraha mattered. That is why every major democratic protest matters. The day a government can simply ignore a peaceful protest without even acknowledging that it exists is the day protest itself begins to lose its meaning. I still remember Anna Hazare’s anti-corruption movement. Whether you agreed with him or not was almost secondary. The movement became impossible to ignore. It dominated the national conversation, forced the UPA government to answer uncomfortable questions, and eventually became one of the defining moments that contributed to the fall of UPA II. That is what protest is supposed to do. Sonam Wangchuk’s protest has now entered its sixteenth day, and yet there has been almost no meaningful response from the government. Not an answer. Not an acknowledgement. Nothing. Agree with him. Disagree with him. Criticise his demands if you want. But at least recognise that a citizen has reached a point where he believes an indefinite hunger strike is the only way left to make himself heard. That reminds me of Professor G.D. Agrawal, the former IIT Kanpur professor who began an indefinite fast demanding that the government honour its promises to clean the Ganga, stop the construction of hydroelectric projects, and curb sand mining along its banks. He died during that protest. And that was it. Imagine that for a moment. A man literally dies while protesting against the government, and the country simply moves on. No real accountability. No meaningful political reckoning. No national introspection. It was as though the protest itself had quietly disappeared. Whether you agreed with Professor Agrawal or not is completely beside the point. A democracy should at the very least acknowledge that such a protest has taken place. It should answer it. It should explain why it agrees or disagrees. Pretending the protest does not exist should never be an option. Which brings me to what I actually think this is about: accountability. Imagine making a mistake at your workplace that delays an important project. You would probably be questioned, reprimanded, maybe even issued a memo. That is how accountability works. It is one of the reasons people take their responsibilities seriously. Now compare that to people occupying some of the highest public offices in the country. In this case, I believe Dharmendra Pradhan should be held accountable for repeated examination failures and paper leak controversies that occurred under his tenure, controversies that affected millions of students and became part of a crisis that has pushed some young people to take their own lives. People immediately ask, “If he resigns, will that solve the problem?” I think that question completely misses the point. Of course one resignation will not solve everything. Accountability has never been about magically fixing a problem overnight. It is about accepting responsibility when something goes wrong. Why do you think most of us try to do our jobs properly? Because we know there are consequences if we fail. Remove accountability, and failure becomes comfortable. Remove accountability, and public office becomes the one workplace where mistakes carry no consequences. If you or I behaved like that in our jobs, we wouldn’t keep them for very long. Yet somehow we expect less from the people entrusted with running the country.

Dharmendra Pradhan Anna Hazare Ministry of Education Paper Leak
