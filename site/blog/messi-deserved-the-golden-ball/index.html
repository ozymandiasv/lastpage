Messi Deserved the Golden Ball

Sometimes the best player doesn’t lift the trophy. That shouldn’t stop him from being recognised as the best player of the tournament.

After a humiliating defeat to Spain, Argentina’s dream of winning the 2026 FIFA World Cup came to an end. Honestly, I’m fine with that. Spain were the better team and deserved to become world champions. Argentina looked unusually poor, unnecessarily aggressive, and Enzo Fernández’s red card for a dangerous tackle summed up their frustrating night. But while I have no complaints about the World Cup trophy going to Spain, I still have one question that I genuinely cannot answer.

How did Rodri win the Golden Ball over Lionel Messi?

If Rodri was the best player on the best team, Messi was the best player of the tournament. Messi was named Player of the Match five times, while Rodri received the award only twice. Messi finished the tournament with 33 key passes, the most by any player, won 60 duels compared to Rodri’s 49, drew the most fouls with 20, completed the most successful take-ons with 28, and created eight big chances, more than anyone else. He also created 25 chances, ahead of Ousmane Dembélé’s 18, finished second in assists with four behind Michael Olise’s seven, and ended the tournament with eight goals, second only to Kylian Mbappé’s ten. Rodri’s biggest statistical advantage came in possession, leading the tournament in completed passes with 204 and final-third passes, numbers that perfectly reflected Spain’s style of football. But football has never been won by possession alone. It is won by players who decide games, create moments from nothing, and change matches when everything seems lost. Throughout this tournament, nobody did that better than Lionel Messi. Even independent performance models seemed to agree. Sky Sports’ Power Rankings rated Messi as the highest-performing player of the tournament, ahead of Kylian Mbappé and Jude Bellingham, while Rodri was considerably lower in their rankings. The IFFHS ratings also placed Messi at the top with an average rating of 8.33, ahead of Mbappé’s 8.15. Looking at both the statistics and the eye test, I genuinely struggle to understand how Messi did not win the Golden Ball. Then again, perhaps that is simply the burden of being Lionel Messi. His career has been so extraordinary that people have almost become numb to it. Every trophy, every award, every record somehow feels expected. At some point, success itself begins to work against you. Another Golden Ball for Messi would almost feel repetitive to many people, but awards should never be decided by career fatigue. They should be decided by what happened on the pitch during that tournament. Forget the numbers for a moment. Watch him play. Watch how he receives the ball, manipulates space, slows the game when everyone else is rushing, and then suddenly accelerates it with a single pass or dribble. Then try to draw a parallel with any footballer, living or dead. Diego Maradona comes close. Ronaldo Nazário comes close. But one was primarily a playmaker and the other primarily a goalscorer. Messi remains the only player I have ever watched who mastered both at the highest level. I’ll soon publish a detailed analytical and statistical comparison of the greatest footballers in history. Maybe then this debate will become a little easier to understand.
