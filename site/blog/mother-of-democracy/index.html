Mother of Democracy

India proudly calls itself the Mother of Democracy. The real test of that claim is not during elections, but when its own students demand accountability.

The students' protest continues, and what initially began as a student movement has finally forced the political establishment to respond. The Indian National Congress has now entered the protest, and unlike the limitations students face while demonstrating, Members of Parliament surprised everyone by staging an unannounced protest outside the Prime Minister's residence. It was a move the government clearly did not anticipate. Union Minister Jitendra Singh later claimed that Rahul Gandhi's request had been accepted within minutes. But what exactly was that request? More importantly, why should the Leader of the Opposition have to request permission in the first place to raise an issue that has brought thousands of students onto the streets? In any functioning democracy, such a discussion should be automatic.

Students Were Answered With Violence

The protest against the government over the paper leak began on Monday, 20 July, and as I wrote yesterday, it quickly descended into violence. Students alleged that police personnel, along with unidentified individuals, assaulted protesters. Tear gas was fired. Lathi charges followed. Journalists on the ground documented students being beaten, in several cases without any visible provocation. Whatever one's political beliefs may be, no government should answer students demanding accountability with force.

Parliament Was Not Allowed to Function

The opposition naturally demanded a discussion in Parliament. According to Rahul Gandhi, when he approached the Speaker, he was told that the government's approval was required before such a discussion could take place. If that is indeed what happened, it is deeply troubling. Parliament does not exist at the mercy of the government. It exists to question the government. If elected representatives cannot immediately debate an issue that has triggered nationwide protests, then one has to ask what Parliament is actually being protected from.

The Prime Minister Who Never Answers

Narendra Modi's silence has become as predictable as it is disappointing. He never speaks to journalists in an unscripted press conference. He never subjects himself to open questioning. Even during moments of national controversy, his silence has become the government's preferred response. How can the Prime Minister of the world's largest democracy remain so distant from public accountability? The office of the Prime Minister is not above democracy. It is accountable to it. Yet India continues to have a Prime Minister who behaves as though answering uncomfortable questions is entirely optional.

Detentions Instead of Accountability

The opposition protest outside the Prime Minister's residence ended with several leaders being detained. That raises a larger question. Thousands of students have protested in Delhi. Opposition MPs have taken the issue from the streets into Parliament and then to the Prime Minister's doorstep. Yet the government's response continues to be defensive rather than accountable. Dharmendra Pradhan still speaks as though responsibility lies somewhere else, while offering little acknowledgment of the government's own failures. Governments are not elected merely to exercise authority. They are elected to answer when things go wrong.

Democracy Cannot Ignore This Forever

The protests continue as I write this, although tensions appear to have eased slightly after J.P. Nadda accepted the protesters' charter of demands. Whether that meeting results in meaningful action remains to be seen. Another concern now looms over Parliament. The politically sensitive Delimitation Bill is expected to be introduced during this session. If large numbers of opposition MPs are suspended amid these protests, serious questions will arise about whether Parliament can genuinely debate legislation of such national importance with full opposition participation. A democracy should never appear afraid of debate.

The Mother of Democracy Must Behave Like One

The government still has an opportunity to correct course. It can listen to students instead of dismissing them. It can answer Parliament instead of avoiding debate. It can accept accountability instead of searching for excuses. Calling India the Mother of Democracy means very little if students are beaten for protesting, Parliament struggles to debate matters of public importance, and the country's highest elected office remains silent. Democracy is not measured by slogans. It is measured by accountability.
