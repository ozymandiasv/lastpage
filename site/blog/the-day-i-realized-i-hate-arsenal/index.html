The Day I Realized I Hate Arsenal

Just a club whose defeats seem to bring me an unreasonable amount of joy

I did not know I hated Arsenal until I watched the Champions League final against Paris Saint-Germain. Even now, writing that sentence feels strange because I had never consciously thought of Arsenal as a club I disliked. I am a Liverpool fan. My football emotions are usually reserved for Liverpool’s victories and defeats. If anything, logic suggests I should dislike Manchester United more. Maybe Tottenham as well. Yet somehow Arsenal managed to occupy a special place in my football brain without me realizing it.

When Arsenal won the Premier League this year, I was not particularly upset. Football works best when different teams win trophies. Dominance gets boring eventually. I looked at Arsenal lifting the league and thought, fair enough. Good for them. They have been knocking on the door for years. But the Champions League final was different. Perhaps it was the intensity of the occasion. Finals have a way of exposing emotions you did not know existed. Suddenly I found myself rooting for Paris Saint-Germain, which is absurd because I do not particularly like PSG either.

I have immense respect for Luis Enrique. What he has done with that team deserves admiration. But PSG as a club has never been easy to support. The whole Lionel Messi, Neymar Jr., and Kylian Mbappé era left a bad taste in my mouth. It felt like a football experiment conducted by accountants. Yet there I was, hoping they would win. Not because I liked PSG, but because I desperately did not want Arsenal to lift the trophy.

That was the moment I knew something was wrong.

Then came the penalties.

Of all the ways Arsenal could lose, penalties felt almost impossible. This is a team that has spent years weaponizing set pieces. Corners have practically become an industrial process under Mikel Arteta. Penalties are usually a strength. Yet somehow the final drifted toward the one scenario where Arsenal looked least likely to fail, and they failed anyway. Watching Gabriel Jesus step up and miss felt like football delivering a joke.

The worst part is that I still cannot explain why it made me so happy.

Maybe every football fan needs one irrational dislike. A club that annoys them for reasons too vague to articulate. A team whose defeats feel disproportionately satisfying. Arsenal might simply be mine. I do not have a grand theory for it. I do not have a list of grievances. All I know is that when the final whistle blew and Arsenal had lost, I celebrated far more than any neutral observer reasonably should have.

And honestly, that realization was almost as entertaining as the match itself. And lol, they have not won a single European Trophy.
