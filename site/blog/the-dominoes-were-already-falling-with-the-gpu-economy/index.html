The Dominoes Were Already Falling with the GPU Economy

A Speculation on Crypto, AI, and the Suspicion That Nothing Was Accidental

I want to be clear about what this is. This is not research. There are no citations, no verified sources or claims, no conclusions and I am prepared to defend this argument in a room full of people who know more than I do. This is just a thought that has been sitting with me, growing heavier the longer I leave it alone. It started with the Epstein files.

The Door That Opened This

Last month's releases had sent the internet into its predictable spiral, allegations flying faster than anyone can verify them. One of the viral claims that caught me thinking was that Epstein wrote the phrase "the Satoshi pseudonym works perfectly" in an email, though this has since been dismissed as fabricated or at least unverified. I looked into it anyway, the way you do when something unsettles you enough to follow it somewhere. And while I found nothing that convincingly tied Epstein to Bitcoin's origins, I found something else. A pattern that had nothing to do with him. A question the Epstein rabbit hole accidentally walked me into. Which is this: who actually built the infrastructure the AI age is running on, and did anyone know in advance that it would be needed? Epstein is not the point. He was just the door.

The Hardware Nobody Questioned

Bitcoin emerged in late 2008 and by 2010 miners had figured out that graphics cards, the kind built for gaming, could mine far faster than ordinary processors. Demand for GPUs exploded almost overnight. Mining operations were buying them in bulk, prices went up, shelves emptied, and companies like NVIDIA and AMD were suddenly under pressure to produce more, scale faster, build better. More processing cores, higher speed memory, greater efficiency, more parallel processing power. And then, almost on cue, another technology arrived needing exactly that. Not something similar. Exactly that. The AI revolution, in its technical requirements, reads like a specification list written for hardware that crypto had already forced into existence. Everything AI needed, crypto had already paid for someone to build. I have tried to write this off as convergence, two industries that happened to need similar things at similar moments. But the more I sit with it the less accidental it feels.

The Warehouses That Were Already There

By late 2025, former Bitcoin mining facilities had signed roughly $65 billion worth of deals with AI companies. Same buildings. Same power infrastructure. Same cooling systems and high speed internet built to run thousands of machines without interruption. Only the task changed. Everything else transferred as if it had always been meant to. That is the detail I cannot shake. The fact that the entire physical pipeline for running large scale compute was funded through a speculative boom, matured quietly over a decade, and then handed off to the next thing that needed it without skipping a beat. It was ready. Almost suspiciously so.

What I Am Not Saying

I am not saying this was orchestrated. I am not drawing a map with lines connecting faces. I am just saying the pieces fit in a way that makes coincidence feel like a lazy explanation. That Bitcoin created the exact economic pressure needed to build what AI would eventually require. That the infrastructure was not just compatible, it was ready and waiting. Maybe separate needs simply aligned. Maybe that is just how technology moves, one wave building what the next one rides. Or maybe someone saw further down the road than the rest of us, and set things in motion early enough that by the time we looked up, the road was already there. We just did not know yet where it was going.
