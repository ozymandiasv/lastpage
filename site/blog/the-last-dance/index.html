The Last Dance

Messi and Ronaldo may be taking their final walk onto football's biggest stage, and the sport still owes us one impossible match

As the internet romantically calls it, the last dance begins again. By the time I wake up tomorrow morning, Argentina might be preparing to face Algeria, and Lionel Messi's final World Cup campaign will officially begin. By the time I go to sleep, Cristiano Ronaldo will begin his. I remember feeling something similar during the last World Cup when Messi finally lifted the trophy and completed football's most famous unfinished story. It felt like the perfect ending, the kind sports rarely gives. Yet here we are again. Another tournament. Another chance. Another opportunity to watch two players who have dominated football for nearly two decades before the curtain finally falls.

The Man Who Survived a God

I admit my bias openly. I want Messi to win it again. If the World Cup settled the debate once, let it settle it forever. That is not disrespect toward Cristiano Ronaldo because my respect for him is immense. Imagine spending your entire career being compared to Lionel Messi and still forcing the world to take the comparison seriously. That alone is one of the greatest achievements in sports history. Most legends are compared against their generation. Ronaldo was compared against someone many people genuinely believe transcends generations altogether. If Messi is football's miracle, Ronaldo is football's greatest act of defiance.

There is something almost unfair about the era Ronaldo was born into. In any other generation, his achievements would have ended every conversation before it began. Hundreds of goals. League titles across countries. Champions League nights that became folklore. International success with Portugal. Yet every achievement was immediately measured against another man doing equally ridiculous things at the same time. Somehow Ronaldo never disappeared beneath that shadow. He stood beside it. Whether you think he won the debate or lost it, surviving it for twenty years is an achievement in itself.

The Magic That Statistics Cannot Explain

I have immense respect for Johan Cruyff, Kaká, Ronaldinho, Ronaldo Nazário, Pelé, and of course Diego Maradona. Every one of them changed football forever, yet the closest I have ever seen to Messi is Maradona. Statistics never explain Messi properly. Goals can be counted. Assists can be counted. Trophies can be counted. What cannot be measured is the feeling that something impossible might happen every time he receives the ball. Even now, entire stadiums still lean forward when he turns. At some point the debate stops becoming analytical and becomes something simpler. If you have watched football long enough, you know what your eyes have been telling you for the last twenty years.

That is why I always struggle when people reduce him to numbers. Messi's greatness was never merely about output. It was about imagination. It was about seeing passes before they existed and creating angles where there were none. Every generation produces legends. Once in a while football produces someone who feels less like a player and more like a natural phenomenon. For me, Messi belongs in that category.

The Script Football Owes Us

What makes this tournament emotional is not merely the possibility of another trophy but the realization that this era is ending. For almost twenty years football revolved around two names. Clubs changed. Managers changed. Tactical systems evolved. New stars emerged every season. Yet somehow Messi and Ronaldo remained above everything else. Yesterday Spain drew while Germany dismantled Curaçao 7-1, and the tournament is slowly beginning to reveal its shape. Like every World Cup, dreams are already being built. Mine is probably the same as everyone else's.

There exists a small possibility that if one of Argentina and Portugal tops their group while the other finishes second, they could end up on opposite sides of the bracket. No Round of 16 meeting. No quarterfinal. No semifinal. Only one possible meeting point. The final. Not another social media debate. Not another comparison graphic. Not another argument over Ballon d'Or awards, goals, or trophies. Just ninety minutes, perhaps more, to decide the final chapter of football's greatest rivalry.

Just Imagine It

Most likely it will never happen because football rarely follows perfect scripts. Injuries happen. Penalties happen. Underdogs emerge and destroy predictions. Yet for now the possibility exists. Lionel Messi against Cristiano Ronaldo. Argentina against Portugal. One final match with the biggest prize in football waiting at the end. The kind of occasion that feels too cinematic to be real and therefore perfectly suited for a sport that occasionally forgets reality altogether.

Long after the records are broken and the debates finally die, what many of us will remember is that we were fortunate enough to witness both of them at the same time. Entire generations waited decades to see one player like this. We somehow got two. Still, if football has one final gift left before this era disappears forever, it knows exactly what the world wants.

Just imagine the final.
