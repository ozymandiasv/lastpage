We Have Gandhi. Next Question.

What the Norway press briefing says about how India handles accountability.

There is a particular kind of discomfort that comes from watching someone answer a question they were never actually asked. It is not quite lying. It is something more practiced than that. A deflection so smooth it almost passes for a response, until you sit with it for a moment and realise nothing was actually said. That is what happened in Oslo.

What Occurred, and What Didn't

Prime Minister Narendra Modi's visit to Norway was, by diplomatic measure, a success. A historic one, even. The first Indian Prime Ministerial visit to the country in 43 years. Trade talks, a joint press statement with Norwegian Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Støre, the works. What did not happen, consistent with a pattern anyone paying attention already knows well, was a press conference where Modi took open questions. That is not new. It is not news. He does not do it here either. What became news was what followed. Norwegian journalist Helle Lyng, working as a commentator with the newspaper Dagsavisen, attempted to ask the Prime Minister a question during the visit. She did not get the chance. The Indian Embassy later personally invited her to a separate press briefing, where MEA Secretary (West) Sibi George fielded questions instead. That briefing is where things got uncomfortable.

The Civilisation Defence

Lyng asked pointed questions about why the international community should trust India, about fundamental rights, about when the Prime Minister would take critical questions from the press. These are not outrageous questions. In Norway, the country that has ranked first on the World Press Freedom Index for years, asking visiting leaders pointed questions is considered standard journalistic practice. Lyng herself said as much. The responses from Sibi George leaned heavily on India's civilisational legacy, its ancient history, Gandhi, yoga, covid vaccine diplomacy. When Lyng tried to press further, she was told not to interrupt. At one point she walked out of the briefing, returning shortly after. The exchange went viral. And then, predictably, so did the abuse directed at her, with sections of Indian social media branding her a foreign spy, a Chinese proxy, a plant. She had to publicly clarify that she was, in fact, just a journalist doing her job.

The Gandhi Problem

Here is what I keep coming back to. When asked about violations of fundamental rights, the response invoked Gandhi. The same Gandhi whose philosophy was built entirely on the idea that unjust laws and unjust systems must be confronted directly, loudly, without waiting for permission. The same Gandhi who would have had quite a lot to say about journalists being shouted down at press briefings. Invoking Gandhi to deflect questions about accountability is not a defence. It is almost its own kind of answer. And the suggestion that citizens who cannot access their basic rights can simply go to court, that the judiciary is there, so what is the problem, deserves more than a moment's pause. Going to court is not a small thing. It costs money, time, and access that most people do not have. The right exists on paper. The distance between the paper and the person is where the real question lives. That is precisely what was being asked. That is precisely what was not answered.

The Number Nobody Mentioned

Norway sits at the top of the 2026 World Press Freedom Index, published by Reporters Without Borders. India sits at 157th out of 180 countries, a drop of six places from the previous year, firmly in the category described as very serious. The index notes the role of concentrated media ownership, legal pressure on journalists, and the broader political environment in arriving at that number. These two countries were in the same room. One of them was asking the questions. The other was explaining that it has Gandhi.

What Whataboutery Costs

I want to be careful here. I am not saying the government has no right to defend India's record. It does. I am not saying every critical question from a foreign journalist is asked in good faith. Some are not. And I am fully aware that diplomatic briefings operate under different rules than open press conferences. But there is a difference between defending a record and refusing to engage with a question. There is a difference between confidence and arrogance. And there is something worth noting in the fact that a journalist asking about press freedom ended the day receiving death threats and spy allegations from anonymous accounts, while the official response was to talk about yoga. Questions do not go away because they are not answered. They just grow quieter, and more people will start asking them.
