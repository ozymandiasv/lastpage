What If Walter Pater Was Right?

Art does not owe us morality, but society has a habit of stealing morality from art anyway

After finishing The Boys, I found myself thinking about Walter Pater, which is perhaps the strangest intellectual route a television show about exploding superheroes can force someone down. Yet the thought refused to leave. Most discussions around The Boys eventually collapse into morality. Was Homelander evil? Was Butcher justified? Was the ending a statement about power, corruption, or human nature? The more I watched people argue over these questions, the more I felt they were looking for something the show was never trying to provide. The Boys is not interested in producing better human beings. It is not trying to inspire virtue. If anything, it spends most of its runtime dismantling the comforting assumption that people are naturally good. That was when Pater came to mind. More than a century ago, he argued that art did not need to justify itself through social usefulness. Art existed for its own sake. Looking at The Boys, I began wondering whether that idea is not only correct, but perhaps necessary.

The Burden We Place Upon Stories

What fascinates me is how determined we are to make stories responsible for things they never volunteered to do. Every time a controversial film releases or a morally ambiguous character becomes popular, the conversation quickly shifts away from the work itself and toward its supposed social consequences. We ask what message the film is sending. We ask whether the story promotes unhealthy ideas. We ask whether audiences might imitate what they see. Hidden beneath all these questions is an assumption that art must somehow justify its existence by producing moral outcomes. It is a strange expectation when you think about it. We do not ask mountains to improve society. We do not ask music to strengthen democracy. Yet we routinely expect novels, films, and television series to function as moral institutions. The aesthetes hated this idea. Pater and Oscar Wilde believed art became smaller the moment it was reduced to utility. A painting was not valuable because it educated. A novel was not valuable because it improved behavior. Art was valuable because it captured something beautiful, profound, disturbing, or true about existence.

The Audience Problem

The difficulty is that Walter Pater was writing about art, while most of us are forced to live among people. If human beings were perfectly rational consumers of stories, perhaps the debate would have ended long ago. Unfortunately, people have an extraordinary talent for learning the wrong lesson from almost everything. Breaking Bad is one of the greatest tragedies ever put on television, yet countless viewers walked away admiring Walter White rather than fearing what he became. Fight Club was intended as a critique of masculine frustration and nihilistic rebellion, yet Tyler Durden evolved into an icon for people who completely missed the criticism. The Joker became a symbol of empowerment despite representing psychological collapse. Even Homelander, who is written with all the subtlety of a nuclear weapon, has somehow gathered genuine admirers. The stories themselves are rarely unclear. The consequences are visible. The warnings are obvious. Yet audiences continuously extract inspiration from characters whose lives are designed to function as cautionary tales. At some point I stopped blaming the stories and started becoming suspicious of the people watching them.

The Characters We Choose to Become

Perhaps the uncomfortable truth is that stories reveal far less about society than the reactions to those stories do. Give a thousand people the same book and you will receive a thousand different interpretations. One reader finishes Crime and Punishment thinking about guilt. Another thinks about rebellion. One viewer watches The Pursuit of Happyness and sees perseverance. Another watches Scarface and sees success. Stories often function less like teachers and more like mirrors. We do not always find wisdom in them. Sometimes we simply find ourselves. This is why I have always struggled with arguments that place the entire burden of interpretation upon artists. A filmmaker can spend three hours showing the destruction caused by greed, violence, or obsession, and somebody will still leave the theatre admiring the confidence of the villain. The artist creates the work, but the audience completes it. Meaning does not arrive fully formed from the creator. It emerges through an interaction between the story and the person consuming it. That interaction is far messier than most cultural critics are willing to admit.

When Art Stops Being Art

There is one exception that continues to bother me. Propaganda. Political movements have always understood something that aesthetes preferred to ignore. Stories influence people. Empires understood it. Religions understood it. Governments understood it. Propaganda does not treat art as an exploration of reality but as a mechanism for directing thought. That is why propaganda feels fundamentally different from ordinary storytelling. A work like 1984 has political implications, but Orwell still trusts readers to think. Propaganda never trusts its audience. It already knows what conclusion it wants them to reach. The purpose is not discovery but conversion. This is where the argument becomes complicated because art and influence are not completely separable. Dickens wanted social reform. Harper Lee wanted to challenge prejudice. Shelley believed poets shaped civilization itself. The history of literature is filled with writers who hoped their work would change the world. The line between artistic expression and social influence has never been as clear as either side of the debate would like.

The Question That Refuses to Leave

The more I think about it, the less interested I become in choosing between Walter Pater and the Romantics. Both seem to have understood different parts of the same truth. Art should not be burdened with a mandatory obligation to improve society. The moment every story must become a lesson, creativity begins to suffocate beneath responsibility. Yet it is equally difficult to pretend that stories have no influence. Human beings have always absorbed values, fears, ambitions, and identities through narrative. Entire civilizations were built upon stories long before they were built upon laws. Perhaps the contradiction is unavoidable. Art owes society nothing, yet society constantly learns from art. Artists cannot control interpretation, yet interpretations have consequences. The story itself may be free, but the audience never arrives empty-handed. We bring our desires, frustrations, insecurities, and ambitions into every work we consume. Maybe that is why I kept thinking about Walter Pater after finishing The Boys. The question was never whether art should teach morality. The question is whether human beings can be trusted with stories that do not.
