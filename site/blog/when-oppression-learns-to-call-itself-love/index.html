When Oppression Learns to Call Itself Love

What Malayalam cinema sees that most cinema looks away from.

A few days ago I watched Ponthan Mada. T.V. Chandran's 1994 film, written from two short stories by C.V. Sreeraman, set in 1940s Kerala under British colonial rule. Mammootty plays Mada, a low-caste toddy tapper who climbs a palm tree every time he wants to speak to his landlord, Sheema Thampuran, played by Naseeruddin Shah, because he is not permitted to enter the house. They talk through a window. Mada hanging from the tree, Thampuran leaning out. And between them, something that genuinely resembles love. That is the film's unbearable genius. Thampuran is not a villain. He is perhaps the most emotionally present person in Mada's life. He notices him, speaks to him, cares for him in the way the system allows. And Mada is proud. Genuinely, deeply proud to be seen by this man. The oppression is not dressed as cruelty. It is dressed as affection. And the system has convinced both of them, oppressor and oppressed alike, that this is simply the natural order of things. That gratitude for scraps is dignity. That love within a hierarchy is still love.

The Same Year, The Other Film

The same year, Mammootty shared his second National Award for Best Actor across two films. The other was Vidheyan, directed by Adoor Gopalakrishnan, one of the great architects of Malayalam parallel cinema. In Vidheyan, Mammootty plays Patelar, a brutal feudal landlord who commits atrocities without hesitation or consequence. There is a scene, quiet and devastating, where a man whose wife Patelar has raped holds her close and feels pride because she smells like Patelar. Pride. Not shame. Not grief. Pride. That scene does something films rarely attempt. It does not ask you to condemn the man. It asks you to understand how a person arrives at that place. How the mind, worn down by years of absolute powerlessness, begins to find identity in the very thing that destroys it. How subjugation, when it goes deep enough and long enough, stops feeling like subjugation and starts feeling like belonging. That Mammootty could inhabit both Mada and Patelar in the same year, that he could move from the meek devotion of one to the cold brutality of the other without a seam showing, is something extraordinary. But leave the performance aside for a moment. Look at what the stories are willing to say.

A Cinema That Does Not Flinch

Adoor Gopalakrishnan's Elippathayam, G. Aravindan's quietly unsettling body of work, the devastating Thaniyavarthanam, directed by Sibi Malayil, where a man's deteriorating mental health is met not with medicine but with rituals and exorcisms because his family is so entrenched in superstition they cannot recognise reality as it dismantles him in front of them. The tragedy there is not just his deterioration. It is everyone around him watching and seeing something else entirely. Then there is Celluloid, about J.C. Daniel, the father of Malayalam cinema. When he screened the first Malayalam film, he arranged a balcony concept so the upper class can sit in the elite chairs. The upper caste found this intolerable. Not because of anything that happened. Because of proximity. Because the idea that someone deemed inferior could occupy a physical space ahead of them, even in a darkened hall, even for two hours of shared entertainment, was an offence the system could not absorb. The very act of watching a film became a battleground for caste hierarchy. Sit with that for a moment.

What Most Cinema Does Instead

Most cinema wants to comfort you. It wants heroes and villains with clear moral architecture, redemption arcs, catharsis, a final scene where something is resolved. It wants you to leave the hall feeling that the world makes sense, or at least that the good people are trying. Malayalam cinema, at its best, gives you none of that. It gives you humans inside systems. Systems so old and so thoroughly internalized that the people inside them have stopped noticing the walls. It gives you Mada on his palm tree, proud to be spoken to. It gives you a man holding his wife and finding comfort in her defilement. It gives you a family conducting rituals around a man who needed a doctor. It does not editorialize. It does not tell you how to feel. It simply holds the frame open long enough for you to see what is actually there.

The Most Terrifying Part

The cruelty is not the hardest thing to look at. The hardest thing is the acceptance. The way oppression, given enough time, stops being something done to people and becomes something people carry inside themselves. The way it becomes culture, and culture becomes identity, and identity becomes something fiercely protected even when it is the very thing causing the wound. These films do not answer the question of how we got here. They just make sure you cannot pretend you did not ask it. And then they leave you with the part that is harder than any answer: the quiet, uncomfortable possibility that we are still here, just with different names for the same walls.
