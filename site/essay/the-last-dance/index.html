The Last Dance

The final World Cup of football’s greatest generation

As the internet romantically calls it, “The Last Dance” begins tomorrow. Messi and Ronaldo may never play another FIFA World Cup. This is it. Thus begins the last dance. By the time I wake up tomorrow, Argentina might already be preparing to face Algeria, and Lionel Messi’s final World Cup campaign may begin. As I write this, I find myself reminiscing about the last World Cup. I was four years younger and broken for many reasons, but amidst all that pain, I experienced a joy unlike anything I had ever felt before. Messi finally lifted the trophy and completed football’s most famous unfinished story. It felt like the perfect ending, the kind sports rarely gives us. Yet here we are again. Another tournament. Another chance. But this isn’t just Messi’s last dance. Cristiano Ronaldo will play his final World Cup too. So will Neymar, Kevin De Bruyne, Mohamed Salah, Luka Modrić, Manuel Neuer, and with them ends perhaps the greatest footballing epoch many of us will ever witness. This is that era’s last dance. But this post is for Messi and Ronaldo, a rivalry that has no equal in the history of football.

A Rivalry Unlike Any Other

I admit my bias openly. I mean, anyone who truly knows the game and has watched Messi play should probably admit theirs too. I want Messi to win it again. People claimed the 2022 World Cup settled the debate once and for all. Let this settle it forever. But it was never just about the World Cup, and I have no disrespect for Cristiano. On the contrary, I have immense respect for what he became. It is no ordinary feat. What he achieved while rivalling Lionel Messi is, in itself, one of football’s greatest accomplishments. Imagine spending your entire career being compared to Messi and still forcing the world to take that comparison seriously. That alone places Cristiano Ronaldo above almost anyone in sporting history. Most legendary rivalries belonged to their own generations. Johan Cruyff had Franz Beckenbauer. Diego Maradona had Michel Platini. Ronaldo Nazário had Zinedine Zidane. Those rivalries defined their eras. But Messi is different. Messi is undeniably a player who transcends generations altogether. If Messi is football’s miracle, Ronaldo is football’s greatest act of defiance. There is something almost unfair about the era Ronaldo was born into. In any other generation, his achievements would have ended every conversation before it even began. Almost a thousand goals. League titles across multiple countries. Champions League nights that became folklore. International success with Portugal. Yet every achievement was immediately measured against another man doing things that often felt beyond explanation. Somehow Ronaldo never disappeared beneath that shadow. He stood beside it. Whether you believe he won the debate or lost it, surviving it for nearly two decades is an achievement in itself.

Why Messi Still Feels Different

I have immense respect for Johan Cruyff, Kaká, Ronaldinho, Ronaldo Nazário, Pelé, and of course Diego Maradona. Every one of them changed football forever, yet the closest I have ever seen to Messi is Maradona. Statistics never explain Messi properly. Goals can be counted. Assists can be counted. Trophies can be counted. What cannot be measured is the feeling that something impossible might happen every single time he receives the ball. Even now, entire stadiums still lean forward when he turns. At some point, the debate stops becoming analytical and becomes something much simpler. If you have watched football long enough, you already know what your eyes have been telling you for the last twenty years. That is why I always struggle when people reduce him to numbers. Messi’s greatness was never merely about output. It was about imagination. It was about seeing passes before they existed and creating angles where there were none. Every generation produces legends. Once in a while, football produces someone who feels less like a player and more like a natural phenomenon. For me, Messi belongs in that category.

One Final Possibility

What makes this tournament emotional is not merely the possibility of another trophy but the realization that this era is ending. For almost twenty years, football revolved around two names. Clubs changed. Managers changed. Tactical systems evolved. New stars emerged every season. Yet somehow Messi and Ronaldo remained above everything else. Yesterday Spain drew while Germany dismantled Curaçao 7–1, and the tournament is slowly beginning to reveal its shape. Like every World Cup, dreams are already beginning to take form. Mine is probably the same as everyone else’s. There exists a small possibility that if one of Argentina and Portugal tops their group while the other finishes second, they could end up on opposite sides of the knockout bracket. No Round of 16 meeting. No quarter-final. No semi-final. Only one possible meeting point. The final. Not another social media debate. Not another comparison graphic. Not another argument over Ballon d’Or awards, goals, or trophies. Just ninety minutes, perhaps more, to decide the final chapter of football’s greatest rivalry. Most likely it will never happen because football rarely follows perfect scripts. Injuries happen. Penalties happen. Underdogs emerge and destroy predictions. Yet, for now, the possibility exists. Lionel Messi against Cristiano Ronaldo. Argentina against Portugal. One final match with the biggest prize in football waiting at the end. The kind of occasion that feels too cinematic to be real and therefore perfectly suited for a sport that occasionally forgets reality altogether.

Just Imagine the Final

Long after the records are broken and the debates finally die, what many of us will remember is that we were fortunate enough to witness both of them at the same time. Entire generations waited decades to see one player like this. We somehow got two. Still, if football has one final gift left before this era disappears forever, it knows exactly what the world wants. Just imagine the final.
