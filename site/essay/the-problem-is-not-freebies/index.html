The Problem Is Not Freebies

We call welfare a freebie and corporate subsidies an investment, then wonder why nobody can have an honest conversation about economics.

A Debate That Never Actually Happens

I came across a post today criticizing freebies and, not long after that, watched Rahul Gandhi defending them. His argument was fairly simple. If governments can provide benefits to people like Gautam Adani and Mukesh Ambani, why is it suddenly wrong when the same government provides benefits to ordinary citizens? Honestly, he isn’t entirely wrong. But I also don’t think he is answering the real question. That, to me, has become the biggest problem with political discussions in India. Nobody seems interested in having the debate that actually matters. One side defends welfare schemes no matter what. The other side opposes them no matter what. Somewhere in between, the entire discussion gets reduced to slogans. What exactly is a freebie? Who deserves public support? At what point does welfare become dependency? At what point does a subsidy become an investment? Those are the questions worth arguing about. Instead, we spend all our time arguing over labels while quietly avoiding the economics underneath them.

A Society Cannot Survive on Advice

Personally, I have never liked the word freebie. The word itself almost assumes guilt. Before we’ve even asked why somebody needs help, we’ve already decided they are taking something they don’t deserve. But that isn’t how most welfare programs exist in the first place. They exist because millions of people simply cannot meet their basic needs through the labour market alone. India may be one of the fastest-growing economies in the world, but growth statistics hide as much as they reveal. Millions of graduates struggle to find work that matches their qualifications. Millions remain trapped in irregular, low-paying jobs. Millions of women contribute enormous amounts of unpaid household labour that never appears in GDP or employment statistics. Disguised unemployment continues to exist across large parts of the economy, and despite recent improvements, we still aren’t fully utilising our workforce. Every time these problems are raised, somebody inevitably says the same thing. Learn a skill. Start a business. Work harder. Adapt. It sounds motivating until it collides with reality. Economies are not self-help books. You cannot motivate millions of people into jobs that simply do not exist. Until those opportunities exist, people still need food, shelter, medicine, and some basic dignity. Welfare isn’t generosity. For many people, it is survival.

Life Support Is Not Development

That is also why I dislike hearing welfare described as an economic strategy. It isn’t one. Giving struggling families financial assistance will not suddenly build industries, increase productivity, solve structural unemployment, or transform the economy. Welfare is much closer to life support than development. It exists because allowing people to collapse into poverty is economically and socially worse than helping them survive. Ideally, governments should create enough employment that fewer people need assistance in the first place. But until that happens, welfare becomes unavoidable. Criticising welfare without asking why so many people need it is like criticising painkillers while refusing to acknowledge the injury.

The Subsidies Nobody Calls Freebies

What fascinates me is how selectively we use the word freebie. A poor family receiving a few thousand rupees is suddenly evidence of fiscal irresponsibility. A farmer receiving support becomes a burden on taxpayers. A student receiving assistance is accused of becoming dependent on the government. But when governments provide subsidised land, tax incentives, infrastructure, regulatory concessions, or other forms of support to large corporations, the language changes overnight. Suddenly it isn’t a freebie anymore. Suddenly it becomes an investment. To be fair, there is a perfectly rational argument behind that. Governments do not support corporations because they are trying to make billionaires richer. They support them because the expectation is that those incentives will create factories, jobs, exports, productivity, and eventually more tax revenue than was spent in the first place. Every successful industrial economy has done some version of this. I don’t disagree with that logic. What I disagree with is pretending that these investments should never be questioned. If public money is being used, then the public has every right to ask what it received in return.

The Return Nobody Wants to Measure

Take the Adani Group as an example. One of India’s largest business empires has publicly reported a workforce measured only in the tens of thousands despite controlling businesses worth hundreds of billions of rupees. My point isn’t that the company should employ millions of people. That would be absurd. My point is much simpler. If governments justify subsidies, incentives, and public support on the grounds that they generate employment and broad economic benefits, then those returns deserve exactly the same scrutiny that welfare schemes receive. We analyse every rupee spent on poor families as though it were a national crisis. Why shouldn’t we apply that same standard to corporate support? If public resources are flowing upwards, the public deserves to know whether the promised jobs, productivity, and economic mobility actually followed. If they did, good. If they didn’t, then calling it an investment instead of a freebie doesn’t magically change what it was.

The Taxpayer Who Loves Subsidies

There is another contradiction I cannot stop thinking about. The average salaried professional spends a remarkable amount of time trying to reduce taxes. Investments are carefully planned. Deductions are maximised. Exemptions are claimed. Every legal provision that reduces tax liability is explored. There is absolutely nothing wrong with that. Most of us would do exactly the same thing. Given the chance, almost everyone prefers keeping more of their own money. What fascinates me is what happens afterwards. The same person who proudly saves fifty thousand, one lakh, or several lakhs through tax planning suddenly becomes furious when somebody else receives a few thousand rupees to cover basic living expenses. Suddenly welfare becomes reckless. Suddenly public spending becomes irresponsible. Suddenly dependency becomes a moral issue. One person’s financial benefit is celebrated as smart financial planning. Another person’s financial benefit is dismissed as a freebie. That difference has far less to do with economics than most people are willing to admit.

The Real Failure

The real failure is not welfare. The real failure is creating an economy where welfare becomes permanently necessary. A healthy economy should not force millions of people to depend on government assistance just to survive. At the same time, it cannot simply abandon them while promising that growth will eventually solve everything. Likewise, support for corporations should never be judged by the size of the company receiving it but by the value created for the public that paid for it. Perhaps that is why I find the entire freebie debate so dishonest. The real question was never whether public money goes to the poor or to the rich. The real question is whether public money is solving public problems. Everything else is branding. We call assistance to struggling citizens a burden. We call assistance to powerful corporations an investment. Then we act surprised when nobody can agree on what a freebie actually is.
