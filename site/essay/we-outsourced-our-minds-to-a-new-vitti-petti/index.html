We Outsourced Our Minds to a New "Vitti Petti"

The television was once the idiot box. Today the smartest screen in our lives remembers, calculates, and thinks for us, leaving us to wonder how much of ourselves we have quietly handed over.

My grandfather used to call the television Vitti Petti, a Malayalam slang that roughly translates to "the dumb box." It was his version of what many English speakers jokingly called the "idiot box." Back then the television was exactly that: a passive screen that showed whatever the broadcaster decided to air. It entertained us, distracted us, occasionally informed us, but it never asked anything of us. Nobody could have imagined that a few decades later the most important screen in our lives would fit inside our pockets, answer our questions before we finished asking them, remember things we had forgotten, guide us through unfamiliar streets, complete our sentences, and quietly become something we depended upon almost every waking hour. Ironically, I was never someone who feared technology. I loved it. Every software update excited me. Every new feature felt like another glimpse into the future. If a device was marketed as "smart," I wanted it. Convenience fascinated me because it felt like progress itself. I never stopped to ask whether every convenience was making me more capable or simply making me less responsible for using my own mind.

That question arrived unexpectedly through something embarrassingly simple. One day I froze while trying to solve a basic addition problem. It wasn't advanced mathematics or some impossible equation. It was the sort of calculation I had performed effortlessly throughout school, the kind of thing I once enjoyed doing mentally for no reason other than the satisfaction of arriving at the answer myself. Yet before I had genuinely tried to think, my hand was already reaching for my phone. The calculation itself was insignificant, but my instinct unsettled me. Somewhere along the way I had stopped trusting my own mind to perform a task it had carried out thousands of times before. My first reaction was no longer to think but to consult a machine. That was the moment I realised the real Vitti Petti was no longer sitting in the corner of the living room. It was glowing inside my pocket. Unlike the old television, this one did not merely entertain me. It remembered for me, calculated for me, corrected my spelling, stored my memories, suggested my next destination, recommended what I should watch, and increasingly reduced the number of moments in which I genuinely had to rely on myself. None of these conveniences seemed dangerous on their own, yet together they had quietly replaced habits that once felt completely natural.

When Convenience Becomes Dependence

Perhaps every generation has surrendered something to technology. We stopped washing clothes by hand because machines did it better. We travelled by cars instead of walking because they were faster. We used tractors instead of ploughs because they were more efficient. Those inventions replaced physical labour, and very few people would argue that humanity became worse because our backs carried less weight. What feels different today is that we are no longer outsourcing only physical effort. We are beginning to outsource cognition itself. Memory has become cloud storage. Navigation has become GPS. Mental arithmetic has become a calculator. Curiosity has become a search engine. Writing has become predictive text. Now, with artificial intelligence becoming part of everyday life, even the earliest stages of thinking risk becoming optional. There is nothing inherently wrong with these tools. In many ways they represent some of humanity's greatest achievements. The danger lies elsewhere. Every time a machine performs a task for us, we practise that task a little less ourselves. Skills rarely disappear overnight. They fade through neglect, quietly enough that we barely notice until one ordinary afternoon when we realise we cannot do something we once considered effortless.

Choosing to Think Again

That realisation changed the way I looked at my own relationship with technology. I silenced almost every notification on my phone and deleted most of the mainstream social media platforms that had trained me to unlock my screen without even thinking about it. For a while I genuinely considered switching to a feature phone, imagining that abandoning the smartphone altogether might somehow restore the attention I felt I had lost. Reality, of course, proved more complicated. Modern life is built around these devices. They hold our tickets, banking, communication, navigation, authentication, and increasingly our work itself. Walking away from them entirely is easier to romanticise than to accomplish. Instead, I chose something smaller but perhaps more sustainable. I made my smartphone quieter. Less demanding. Less eager to interrupt every idle moment. Most importantly, I began forcing myself to calculate simple things in my head again. Grocery totals. Discounts. Small additions that I previously would have handed over to a calculator without hesitation. I still make mistakes, and sometimes I still reach for my phone out of habit, but every correct answer feels strangely satisfying because it reminds me that the ability never disappeared. It had simply become dormant through disuse.

The Mind We Are Quietly Giving Away

I suspect I am far from alone in this. Most of us have experienced similar moments without recognising what they represent. We no longer remember phone numbers because our contacts remember them for us. We struggle to navigate familiar places without GPS because we stopped paying attention to the roads years ago. We interrupt conversations to verify facts that could have waited until later because uncertainty itself has become uncomfortable. None of these habits are catastrophic in isolation, but together they reveal something deeper about the relationship we are building with technology. Convenience has slowly become dependency, and dependency has become so ordinary that we mistake it for progress. This is why conversations about artificial intelligence fascinate me. People often ask whether AI will replace human intelligence, but I wonder if that process has already begun in much smaller ways. Perhaps replacement does not happen dramatically. Perhaps it begins every time we choose instant assistance over a few seconds of independent thought. AI is not dangerous because it can think faster than we can. It is dangerous only if we gradually lose the desire to think at all.

The Question That Remains

I am not arguing that we should reject technology or return to some romanticised past where life was slower simply because it was harder. Technology has transformed medicine, education, communication, and countless other parts of human life for the better. Artificial intelligence will undoubtedly do the same in ways we cannot yet imagine. The question, however, is not whether our tools should become smarter. Of course they should. The question is whether we are allowing ourselves to become intellectually weaker simply because our tools have become intellectually stronger. My grandfather laughed at the television because he believed it encouraged people to sit back and watch instead of doing something more meaningful. He had no way of knowing that the real Vitti Petti was still waiting to be invented. It would not ask us merely to watch. It would remember for us, calculate for us, write for us, recommend for us, and perhaps one day even think for us. The future of technology does not worry me nearly as much as the possibility that, somewhere along the way, we might quietly forget how to think for ourselves.
